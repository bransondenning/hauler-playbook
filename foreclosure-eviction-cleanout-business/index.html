Foreclosure and Eviction Cleanout Business: The Operator's Playbook for Bank, Realtor & PM Accounts

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Most haulers chase one-off garage cleanouts off the internet. The smart ones quietly lock up a different kind of work: trashouts. A foreclosure and eviction cleanout business serves banks, asset managers, realtors, property managers (PMs) and attorneys who need a property emptied, swept and photo-documented on a deadline. The jobs are bigger, the pay is steadier, and once you're on a vendor list, the same handful of people send you work month after month.

This is the work my own crew leans on when the residential phone goes quiet. Below is the real playbook: who buys, how to get on their list, how to price a trashout, how to stay out of legal and emotional trouble, and how to turn one good job into a recurring account.

What a trashout actually is

"Trashout" is industry slang for clearing everything out of a property after the occupant is gone, whether they left voluntarily, were evicted, or the home went back to the lender through foreclosure. You're not appraising or storing anything in most cases - you're removing all personal property and debris, hauling it to the transfer station, and leaving a clean, empty shell ready for a cleaning crew, lockbox and listing.

Two flavors dominate:

  • Foreclosure / REO trashouts. The bank now owns the home (REO = real estate owned). An asset manager or field-services company orders the cleanout, often with a strict per-square-foot cap and required before/after photos.
  • Eviction cleanouts. A landlord or PM regained possession through the court. The unit still has a former tenant's belongings, sometimes including food, pet waste, and biohazard-adjacent messes. Speed matters because the unit can't re-rent while it's full.

Attorneys sit on the edge of both - eviction attorneys, estate attorneys, and bankruptcy trustees all need a reliable hauler they can hand a key to. Treat the legal angle as a referral channel, not a separate business.

Who actually pays you

You won't usually deal with a "bank" as a faceless institution. The decision-makers are specific people:

  • Asset managers / field-services firms that manage REO inventory for lenders. They send standardized work orders.
  • Property managers running rental portfolios - the single best source of repeatable cleanout volume. If you only chase one channel, chase this one. We break the approach down in our guide to property manager cleanout accounts.
  • Realtors / REO agents who get a listing that's full of junk and need it gone before photos.
  • Eviction and estate attorneys who refer their landlord clients to a hauler they trust.

The common thread: these are professionals who repeat. A homeowner cleans out a garage once a decade. A PM with 200 doors has turnover every single month.

How to get on the vendor list

This isn't won with a flyer. It's won with reliability and a five-minute phone call. Here's the sequence that works:

  1. Build a credibility kit. A one-page capability sheet, your insurance certificate, before/after photos, and a flat statement that you carry general liability and commercial auto and provide photo documentation on every job.
  2. Call, don't email. Ask the front desk who handles "make-ready and cleanout vendors." Get a name. PMs especially live on the phone.
  3. Lead with their pain. "I clear evicted and foreclosed units fast, send before/after photos, and invoice net-15. When your next unit turns, I can be there in 48 hours." That sentence wins more accounts than any ad.
  4. Prove it once. Nail the first small job - on time, clean, photos in their inbox before they ask. The second job comes without a bid.

The cheap channels that actually work

You still want inbound while you build accounts. Set up a free Google Business Profile and ask every happy customer to leave a review from their own phone and account - never post reviews yourself or from a shop device. A Facebook business Page plus active posting in local landlord and "for rent" groups gets you in front of small landlords directly. Do not list cleanout "services" on Facebook Marketplace - it's built for selling items, not services, and service posts get pulled. On Craigslist, a post in the labor / moving category runs about $5 (it is not free) and reaches the small-landlord crowd. More low-cost plays are in our junk removal marketing ideas rundown.

How to price a trashout

Trashouts price differently than a normal pickup. You're quoting a whole property, sight-sometimes-unseen, against the same two cost drivers as any junk job: volume (how full your trailer or truck gets) and disposal weight (what the dump charges by the ton). Our full junk removal pricing guide covers the math; here's the trashout-specific version.

Three pricing models you'll use:

  • Volume / load-based. Your default. Price by the fraction of the trailer filled, same as residential, but quote the whole house as multiple loads.
  • Flat per-property bid. What PMs and agents prefer. Walk it (or judge from photos), estimate loads and dump fees, add labor and a margin buffer for surprises.
  • Per-square-foot cap. REO/field-services orders often cap reimbursement (commonly framed per square foot). Know the cap before you commit, or you'll work for free.

Always pad a trashout. Evicted units hide problems: a packed garage you couldn't see, a fridge full of rotten food, three months of trash in a back bedroom. Build a contingency into every flat bid.

Handle the appliances and hazards right

Trashouts are full of refrigerators, freezers, window AC units and dehumidifiers. These contain refrigerant, and under federal rules the refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA-certified technician before disposal. Never cut the lines or vent it yourself - that's both illegal and dangerous. Build a relationship with an appliance recycler or certified tech and factor that handling into your price.

You'll also meet the ugly stuff: spoiled food, pet and human waste, needles, mold, and the occasional mystery in the garage. Carry gloves, masks, and contractor bags as standard kit, and have a line on a licensed disposal route for anything truly hazardous (paint, solvents, chemicals). Know what your transfer station will and won't take before the trailer is loaded.

The sensitivity factor

This is what separates a hauler who keeps the account from one who loses it. A foreclosure or eviction is often the worst week of someone's life. Sometimes the former occupant is still around, or a neighbor is watching, or family photos are sitting in a box on the curb.

  • Stay professional and quiet. No jokes about the contents, no photos for social media, no commentary.
  • Follow the work order on personal property. Some jurisdictions and contracts require holding or documenting certain belongings. Do exactly what the order says - when in doubt, photograph and ask, don't toss.
  • Confirm legal possession before you touch anything. For evictions, the landlord or PM must have legal possession (a completed court process). You're a vendor, not the enforcer - get written authorization on the work order.
  • Be discreet on documents. Shred or set aside obvious financial/personal paperwork rather than dumping it loose.

Handle a hard job with dignity and the PM remembers. That's how a one-off becomes a standing account.

Turn one job into recurring revenue

The whole point of this niche is repeat work. After a clean first job:

  • Ask for the next one directly. "Want me on your standing vendor list? I'll hold a 48-hour turnaround for your units."
  • Offer net-15 or net-30 invoicing. Professionals expect terms, not cash-on-the-spot. It signals you're a real vendor.
  • Send photo documentation every time, unasked. Before/after shots protect the PM with the owner and protect you against disputes.
  • Stay top of mind. A quarterly "any turns coming up?" text keeps you first-call.

One PM with steady turnover can be worth more than fifty Craigslist leads. Stack three or four of those and you have a base that doesn't depend on ads. The closely related estate cleanout business runs on the same referral muscle - attorneys, realtors and PMs who already trust you will hand you both.

The boring stuff that keeps you legal

Two things people constantly confuse. First, an LLC is not a license. An LLC is a business structure that protects your personal assets; it does not grant permission to haul. Check your city/county for any local hauling or business permits separately.

Second, carry real insurance. General liability typically runs about $40-$90 a month for a small hauler, and you'll need commercial auto on the vehicle pulling the trailer - personal auto won't cover a work vehicle in a claim. No serious PM or asset manager adds an uninsured vendor to their list; the certificate of insurance is your ticket in. The full breakdown is in junk removal license and insurance.

On equipment: a dump trailer or a solid enclosed trailer behind a 3/4-ton truck handles most trashouts without the overhead of a box truck. Your first 30 days

  1. Get insured and structured. Bind GL and commercial auto; form your LLC; check local permits.
  2. Build the credibility kit. One-pager, insurance certificate, a few before/after photos (even from cleaning your own garage to start).
  3. Stand up your free channels. Google Business Profile, Facebook Page, and a $5 Craigslist labor post.
  4. Make 20 calls. PMs, REO agents, and eviction attorneys. Ask for the vendor-list person by name.
  5. Nail one job and ask for the next. On time, clean, photos sent before they ask.
