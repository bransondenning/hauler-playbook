Garage Cleanout Service Pricing: How to Quote, Sell, and Run the Bread-and-Butter Job

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

The garage cleanout is the job that pays the light bill. It is the most common residential call we get, the easiest to quote, and the fastest to turn into a five-star review and a referral. If you are building a hauling business, this is the work you want to be excellent at before you chase estate cleanouts or commercial accounts. Get garage cleanout service pricing right and the rest of the business gets a lot easier.

This is how we actually quote, sell, and run garages in the field - the numbers, the scripts, and the upsells that turn a $250 job into a $400 job without anyone feeling squeezed.

How garage cleanout service pricing actually works

Forget hourly. Customers hate hourly because it feels open-ended, and you will lose money on it the first time a "quick garage" turns into four hours of disassembling shelving. The entire industry runs on volume-based flat pricing - you charge by how much space the junk takes up in your truck or trailer, and the price is all-in (labor, hauling, dump fees, and sweep-out).

For a garage, you are almost always selling a fraction of a load up to a full load. Here is a realistic 2026 price card for a mid-size US market. Adjust up 15-25% for high-cost metros and down a touch for rural areas.

Quote from a photo, then confirm on site

The fastest-closing operators quote from a photo. Tell the customer to text you a picture from the garage doorway. You will be able to size it within one tier almost every time. Quote a range ("looks like a half to three-quarter load, so $300 to $400 depending on what's behind that workbench") and lock the exact number when you arrive. This sets expectations, weeds out tire-kickers, and gets you off the phone fast.

How to sell the garage cleanout on the phone

Most garage leads are won or lost in the first ninety seconds. People are embarrassed about the mess and nervous about getting nickel-and-dimed. Your job is to make it feel safe and simple.

  • Lead with the flat price, not the hourly. "It's one flat price, all-in - labor, hauling, dump fees, and we sweep up after. No surprises." This single line beats most of your competitors who fumble around with "well, it depends."
  • Anchor against the chains. "The big national companies usually quote $600 to $800 for a two-car garage. We do the same haul for less because we're local and lean."
  • Sell the same-day slot. Garages are emotional - people decide to do it and want it gone now. "I've got a slot open this afternoon if you want your garage back today."
  • Handle the "can you just give me an exact price" with the photo move. "Text me a photo from the doorway and I'll lock your number in under an hour."

Running the job: a clean garage cleanout, start to finish

A two-person crew should clear a typical half-to-three-quarter garage in 60 to 90 minutes. Speed comes from a system, not from hustle.

  1. Walk it with the customer first. Confirm what stays and what goes. Tag anything questionable - that beat-up dresser in the corner might be Grandma's. Two minutes here saves you a painful "wait, that wasn't supposed to go" moment at the dump.
  2. Lock the final price out loud, in writing. "So we're at three-quarters of a load, $375 flat, plus the $45 for the freezer - $420 total, sound good?" Get the yes before you lift anything.
  3. Load heavy and flat first. Appliances, furniture, and lumber go in the truck first as your base. Boxes and loose junk fill the gaps. A well-packed load is the difference between one trip and two.
  4. Sweep and show. Always broom-sweep the empty garage. Then have the customer stand at the door and look. That "wow" moment is what earns the review and the referral.
  5. Collect on the spot. Tap-to-pay, Venmo, or cash before you pull away. Same-day payment is one of the biggest cash-flow advantages this business has.

The upsells that turn $275 into $425

Garages are loaded with add-on items the customer "forgot" to mention. Price these as clear flat add-ons, not vague upcharges, and quote them before you load:

  • Appliances: +$45 each for a fridge, freezer, washer, or dryer. Critical: anything with refrigerant - fridges, freezers, some window AC units, dehumidifiers - requires EPA-certified refrigerant recovery before disposal. Never cut a line or vent it. Either get certified, partner with a certified recycler, or charge the add-on to cover proper drop-off. Cutting corners here is an actual federal violation, not a gray area.
  • Mattresses: +$25 each (many transfer stations charge a separate mattress fee, so pass it through).
  • Tires: +$10-$15 each - garages always have a stack of old tires.
  • TVs and electronics: +$25 for e-waste handling.
  • Heavy/specialty items: hot tubs, safes, riding mowers, and workbenches run $150-$400 depending on weight and disassembly.
  • Access upcharges: +$50 for stairs, +$30 for a long carry from the garage to the truck. Mention these on the phone, never at the curb.

Two or three honest add-ons regularly lift a job by $90-$150. The trick is transparency - quote them upfront so it never feels like a bait-and-switch.

Turn every garage into marketing: the before/after machine

The garage cleanout is the single best content generator in this business because the transformation is so dramatic. Build the habit on every single job:

  • Shoot before and after from the exact same spot. Stand at the door, snap the chaos, then snap the empty swept garage from the same angle. Identical framing makes the slider/side-by-side hit hard.
  • Post natively, the right way. Build a Facebook business Page and post before/afters there and into local community Facebook groups - that's where neighbors actually hire. Do not try to list your service on Facebook Marketplace as an item; Marketplace forbids service listings and will pull them. Use the Page and groups instead.
  • Use Craigslist correctly. A Craigslist services post is $5 (it is not free) and belongs in the labor / moving category. Repost your best before/after every few days to stay near the top.
  • Get the Google review on the customer's own phone, on site. The "wow" moment at the swept garage is peak goodwill. Hand them nothing - ask them to pull up your Google Business Profile on their device and leave a quick review while you load the last items. Reviews from the customer's own phone are what actually stick and feed your local ranking.

What you legally need before you take the first garage

You can start small, but do not skip the basics. A few realities operators get wrong:

  • An LLC is not a business license. An LLC is a legal structure for liability protection. A business license is a separate local/state registration to legally operate. You may need both - check your city and county requirements.
  • General liability insurance runs roughly $40-$90/month for a small hauler and protects you if you ding a customer's garage door or drywall (you will, eventually).
  • Commercial auto insurance is separate - your personal policy will not cover the truck or trailer once you are hauling for pay. This is non-negotiable.

Is the garage cleanout actually worth your time?

Run the math on a typical half-load garage. You charge $325 flat. Your costs: maybe $40-$70 in dump fees, $15 in fuel, and 75 minutes of two people's time. That leaves strong gross margin on a job you can run two or three of per day. Add one appliance and a mattress and you're often clearing more on a single afternoon than a full retail shift.

These are example numbers, not a promise - your dump fees, drive times, and market rates will vary, and income is never guaranteed. But the structure is why the garage cleanout is the backbone job: high demand, fast turnaround, low complexity, and built-in marketing. Master it first, then expand into bigger work like estate and rental cleanouts.
