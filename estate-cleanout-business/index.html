How to Start an Estate Cleanout Business (2026 Operator's Guide)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company, and the single most profitable niche we ever leaned into wasn't garage cleanups or appliance hauls. It was estate cleanouts: clearing out an entire house after someone passes, downsizes into assisted living, or finally lets family deal with decades of accumulation. These jobs pay two to five times what a single-item pickup does, the leads are repeatable, and most of your competition is too squeamish or too disorganized to do them well.

This is the playbook for adding estate, hoarder, and downsizing cleanouts to a hauling operation, or building a business around them from day one. If you're starting from zero, read how to start a junk removal business first for the legal and equipment basics, then come back here for the niche.

Why estate cleanouts pay more than regular junk removal

A standard junk job is one customer, one decision, one truckload. An estate cleanout is a whole house: kitchen, garage, basement, three bedrooms, the shed out back, and the attic nobody's opened since the Clinton administration. You're often filling multiple loads over one or two days. That alone stacks the ticket.

But the real reason it pays is the buyer. The person hiring you usually isn't paying out of their own pocket in the way a homeowner is. It's the estate's money, the adult child who lives three states away and just wants it handled, the realtor who needs the house empty to list it Thursday, or the probate attorney working through an estate. These people are buying speed and relief, not the cheapest hourly rate. They will pay a premium to make a stressful problem disappear in one phone call.

In our experience, a full-house cleanout in a typical US market runs anywhere from $800 to $4,500 depending on volume and access, with hoarder-level jobs going higher. Compare that to a $150 minimum pickup. You don't need many of these a month to change the math. Where the estate cleanout leads actually come from

Homeowners search "junk removal near me." The people who feed you estate work don't search at all, they refer. Your job is to become the name five specific types of professionals keep in their phone. Here's where to spend your outreach time, roughly in order of payoff:

  • Realtors and listing agents. An agent with a hoarder listing or an inherited property has a dead deal until the house is empty. Clear it fast and clean and you become their permanent cleanout person. One productive agent can send you a job a month.
  • Estate-sale and auction companies. They sell the valuable stuff, then need everything that didn't sell hauled away. The leftovers are guaranteed volume, and the timing is predictable: the day after the sale ends. Partner with two or three of these and you've got a recurring pipeline.
  • Senior living and assisted-living facilities. When someone moves into care, their house has to be emptied, often on a tight move-in deadline. Move-in coordinators and discharge planners refer cleanout help constantly. They're looking for someone gentle and reliable, not the lowest bidder.
  • Probate and elder-law attorneys. Executors are legally responsible for settling an estate, and clearing the property is on their list. Attorneys refer the vendors who make them look good. Drop off a card, do one job flawlessly, and you're on the shortlist.
  • Senior move managers and downsizing specialists. A whole profession exists to help older adults move. They sort and pack; they hire someone else to haul. That someone should be you.

The outreach itself is simple but it's not free of effort. A short intro email, a follow-up text, and an actual in-person drop-by beat any ad you could run. This is the same B2B muscle you use for landlord work, and it's worth building deliberately. How to price a whole-house cleanout

We price almost everything by flat rate based on how much of the trailer the job fills, not by the hour. Hourly invites haggling and punishes you for being efficient. A flat price by volume is honest, fast to quote, and protects your margin. Our standard load card looks like this:

Load sizeExample flat rate
Minimum pickup$150
Quarter trailer$175
Half trailer$275
Three-quarter trailer$350
Full trailer$425

These are example figures from our market, all-in (labor, hauling, dump fees, and sweep-out). Your local dump rates and cost of living will move them. The point is the structure: clear tiers a customer understands instantly. For a full walkthrough of building your own card, see our junk removal pricing guide.

A whole-house estate cleanout is just multiple full loads stacked, plus a few adjustments unique to the niche:

  • Quote from photos and a walkthrough. For a single-room job, a customer texts a photo and gets a flat price within the hour. For a whole house, you walk it in person (or get a video tour) and estimate total trailer loads. Always pad for the stuff hidden in closets, crawlspaces, and that one terrifying basement corner. Cleanouts almost always hold more than they look.
  • Bundle the loads, don't itemize the day. Quote "$2,400 to clear the entire house, everything gone, floors swept" rather than nickel-and-diming each room. The buyer wants one number and an empty house.
  • Charge for the hard stuff. Stairs, long carries from a back bedroom to the curb, and packed-out hoarder conditions all add real labor. Build upcharges into the quote rather than eating them. A typical stairs or long-carry add runs $30 to $50 per flat.
  • Add-ons by item. Refrigerators and freezers, mattresses, TVs, and tires carry small per-item fees because they cost you extra at the scale or the recycler. A fridge add of $45 is normal because of how it has to be disposed of, which we'll get to.

The donation and sensitivity angle that wins the job

Here's the part most haulers miss, and it's exactly what closes estate work. You are not clearing a garage. You are handling the physical remains of someone's life while their family is grieving or overwhelmed. How you show up matters as much as your price.

Two things win these jobs over a cheaper competitor:

You handle it with respect. Watch for photos, documents, jewelry, and keepsakes mixed into the junk. Set anything that looks personal aside and flag it for the family before it goes in the trailer. Show up clean, speak softly, and never rush a grieving daughter through her dad's workshop. We've earned repeat referrals from a single family simply because we slowed down and asked before tossing a shoebox of letters.

You donate what you can. Families hate the idea of perfectly good furniture, clothing, and household goods going to the landfill, especially when it belonged to someone they loved. Build relationships with a couple of local charities and thrift stores, and tell the customer up front: "Anything usable, we donate, and we'll get you the receipt." That donation receipt has real value to an estate for tax purposes, and the gesture itself is often what tips the decision your way. It costs you a little routing time and buys you the job plus the referral.

Put both of these in your pitch and your reviews. "Respectful, donated what they could, handled everything" is the kind of review that makes the next grieving family call you instead of the chain.

What to refuse, flag, or charge extra for

Estate and hoarder houses hide things a normal junk job doesn't. Knowing what to walk away from protects you legally and financially:

  • Refrigerators, freezers, and AC units. These contain refrigerant that must be recovered by an EPA-certified technician before the unit is scrapped. Never cut a refrigerant line yourself. Either partner with a certified recycler or charge an add-on that covers proper handling. This is why fridges carry an extra fee.
  • Hazardous materials. Paint, solvents, motor oil, propane tanks, pesticides, and old chemicals are not regular trash and most transfer stations won't take them in your load. Quote these as "household hazardous waste, handled separately" or direct the family to the local drop-off. Don't bury them in a trailer.
  • Biohazard and extreme hoarder conditions. Animal waste, mold, or anything that needs PPE and remediation is a different trade. Know your limit and refer it out rather than putting your crew at risk.
  • Anything that might have value. In an estate, the family or executor decides what stays. Never assume the old coins, firearms, or the box in the attic are trash. Flag valuables and let the responsible party make the call, in writing if it's a big estate.
  • Firearms and ammunition. Stop and get the executor's direction. There are legal handling rules, and you don't want to be the reason a probate gets complicated.

When in doubt, the rule is simple: if it's regulated, dangerous, or possibly valuable, you flag it and quote it separately. That discipline is also what makes attorneys and realtors trust you with bigger jobs.

The legal and insurance baseline

Before you take money for hauling anyone's property, get the basics right. Carry general liability insurance, which typically runs $40 to $90 a month for a small hauling operation, because you're working inside people's homes and one scratched hardwood floor can wipe out a job's profit. Form an LLC for liability protection, but understand an LLC is not a license. Many cities and counties require a separate hauler permit or business license, and some require you to be registered to use the transfer station. Check your local rules. A realistic first 90 days

You don't need a huge launch to break into this. Here's the sequence we'd run if we were starting the estate niche today:

  1. Lock the basics: insurance, permit/license, a dump account, and a trailer that can swallow a full load.
  2. Build the flat-rate card so you can quote a whole house in two minutes instead of guessing.
  3. Set up two donation outlets and learn what they will and won't accept.
  4. Make your referral list: ten realtors, three estate-sale companies, two senior-living facilities, and two probate attorneys in your area. Visit them in person.
  5. Do the first job flawlessly, donate what you can, get the receipt to the family, and ask for the review and the referral.

Estate cleanouts reward operators who are organized, calm, and genuinely decent to deal with. The price premium is real, but it's earned by handling a hard day for a family better than the next hauler would. Do that consistently and the referral sources above will keep your trailer full.
