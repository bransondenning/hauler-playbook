Junk Removal Equipment and Tools List: The Complete Startup Kit

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company, and the question we get most from people trying to start one isn't about marketing or trucks. It's "what do I actually need to put in the back of the truck on day one?" The honest answer is: less than you think to start, and more than you think to do it without wrecking your back or scratching a customer's hardwood floors.

This is the full junk removal equipment and tools list we'd hand a brand-new operator. For each category I'll tell you what the item is for, the budget option, and the pro option, plus rough 2026 US prices. You can launch a working setup for around $400-$700 if you already own a truck or trailer, or kit out a serious rig for $1,500-$2,500. Spend on the things that protect your body and the customer's property first; everything else can wait until the jobs are paying for it.

The non-negotiables: what you need before your first job

If money is tight, buy this short list and nothing else. Everything here pays for itself in one or two jobs by preventing injury, property damage, or a back-throwing-out that puts you on the couch for a week.

Hand truck and appliance dolly

This is the single most important tool you own. A standard hand truck (the L-shaped two-wheeler) moves boxes, totes, and small furniture. An appliance dolly is the heavier-duty version with a strap and stair climbers for fridges, washers, dryers, and safes.

  • Budget: a basic 600-lb hand truck runs $40-$70.
  • Pro: a convertible hand truck that goes from upright to four-wheel cart is $130-$200, and a dedicated appliance dolly with a ratchet strap and stair-climber wheels is $150-$300. If you plan to do any appliance removal work, the appliance dolly is not optional.

Moving straps and ratchet straps

Two different tools that both get called "straps." Forearm forearm/shoulder moving straps ($25-$40) use leverage so two people can carry a couch or a deadlift fridge up stairs without grip strength being the limit. Ratchet straps and cam straps ($20-$50 for a set) secure the load in the truck or trailer so nothing flies out on the highway, which is both a safety issue and a ticket if you lose debris.

Moving blankets and a tarp

Moving blankets ($40-$80 for a 6-12 pack) protect walls, door frames, and railings on the way out, and they protect items you're reselling or donating. A heavy-duty tarp ($25-$60) covers an open load so you stay legal and don't shed junk down the road. If you run an open trailer, a cargo net or tarp is mandatory in most states.

PPE: gloves, eye protection, boots, masks

You will reach into things you can't see. Cut-resistant work gloves ($15-$30 a pair, buy several), safety glasses ($5-$15), an N95/respirator for dusty or moldy cleanouts ($1-$30 depending on whether it's disposable or a half-mask), and steel-toe or composite-toe boots ($80-$160) are the baseline. For hoarding cleanouts add Tyvek suits ($5-$12 each) and nitrile gloves. PPE is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.

The "makes the job faster" tier

Once you've done a handful of jobs, these tools turn a two-hour grind into a 45-minute job. That speed is the whole game, because junk removal is priced by the load, not the hour, so every minute you save is margin.

Reciprocating saw (sawzall)

A corded or battery reciprocating saw is how you turn a bulky sectional, a swing set, a shed, or a particleboard wardrobe into pieces that fit through a doorway and stack flat in the truck. Bigger items broken down means more loads per trip and fewer trips to the transfer station.

  • Budget: a corded sawzall is $50-$90, plus a multi-pack of demolition blades ($15-$25). Wood-and-nail blades are what you want.
  • Pro: a brushless 18V/20V cordless sawzall with two batteries, $150-$280. Cordless wins when you're 60 feet from the nearest outlet, which is most basements and back yards.

Power drill / impact driver

For disassembling bed frames, desks, treadmills, and anything bolted to a wall or deck. A cordless drill/driver combo kit is $90-$200. Get a basic bit and socket set ($20-$40) to go with it. A treadmill that won't fit through the door comes apart in ten minutes with the right driver.

Hand tools and demolition tools

  • Pry bar / wrecking bar ($15-$30) for decking, nailed-down items, and trim.
  • Bolt cutters ($20-$50) for locks, chain, and fencing.
  • Sledgehammer and a regular hammer ($25-$50 total).
  • Utility knives plus a big pack of blades ($15-$25). You'll go through blades fast.
  • Adjustable wrench, channel locks, and a screwdriver set ($30-$60).
  • Furniture sliders ($10-$20) for dragging heavy pieces across floors without scratching them.

Trash bags, bins, and a shovel

Contractor-grade 3-mil bags ($20-$40 a box) hold up to broken glass and sharp debris where kitchen bags rip. Add a flat-blade and a square-point shovel ($25-$50 each), a push broom and dustpan ($25), and a few totes or buckets for sorting scrap. Leaving the site cleaner than you found it is how you earn the five-star review and the repeat call.

Special-case gear you'll need eventually

Refrigerant appliances

Fridges, freezers, window AC units, and dehumidifiers contain refrigerant. Under EPA rules, the refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA Section 608-certified technician before the unit is disposed of or recycled. Never cut, puncture, or "just bleed" the lines yourself, that's illegal and dangerous. In practice most operators move the appliance intact and drop it at a recycler or scrap yard that handles certified recovery. Build that fee into your price.

Mattresses, hot tubs, and heavy specialty items

Mattresses get charged a disposal surcharge at most facilities and many areas now require recycling, see our notes on mattress disposal. Hot tubs need the sawzall, a sledge, and usually a second person; it's a profitable niche if you price it right. A folding furniture dolly with four casters ($30-$60) saves your back on anything you can roll instead of carry.

Scrap and resale separation

Keep a magnet and a few labeled totes so you can pull steel, aluminum, copper, and brass out of every load. Scrap and resale can add a real second income stream on top of the haul fee, and the only "equipment" it requires is a few bins and the discipline to sort.

The "tools" that aren't tools: paperwork, insurance, and getting found

Your physical kit is maybe half of what makes a junk removal business work. The other half is the boring stuff that keeps you legal and brings the phone calls.

  • Insurance. General liability typically runs about $40-$90 a month for a small operation, and if you're hauling in a vehicle you'll also need a commercial auto policy. Forming an LLC protects you legally but an LLC is not a business license, those are separate, and most cities require a local business license on top. Details in our license and insurance guide.
  • Quotes and invoices. A simple quote-and-invoice template and a clear price card so every job is consistent. Pricing by load volume is the standard, our pricing guide walks through how to set rates that actually cover dump fees and your time.
  • Getting found. A free Google Business Profile is the highest-ROI marketing a local junk hauler has. Ask happy customers to leave a review from their own device after the job, no shortcuts and no guaranteed ranking, but consistent real reviews compound over months. Pair it with a Facebook page and posting in local buy/sell/community groups. Note that Facebook Marketplace forbids listing services as items, so a page plus groups is the right move there. On Craigslist, a services post in the labor/move category costs $5; it's cheap, not free.

None of this gear is exotic. A lot of it is in your garage already. The discipline is buying in the right order, body protection and property protection first, speed tools second, specialty gear as the jobs demand it, so you're never out more cash than the work can pay back. Do that and a typical operator can be fully equipped before the second or third paying job covers the bill. Income varies a lot by market, hustle, and how you price; treat any numbers you see (here or anywhere) as examples, not promises.
