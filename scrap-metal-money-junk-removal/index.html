Making Money From Scrap Metal in Junk Removal: The Hidden Profit Most Haulers Ignore

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Here's a number that surprised me my first year hauling: a single curbside dishwasher, a water heater, and a busted bed frame netted me an extra $34 at the scrap yard on top of the $180 the customer already paid me to take it away. Same trip. Same truck. Same fuel. That's the part nobody tells you about this business. The customer pays you to remove the junk, and then a chunk of that junk pays you a second time.

Scrap metal won't replace your hauling revenue. But over a year, the haulers who sort and sell scrap consistently out-earn the ones who run everything straight to the transfer station and pay tipping fees on metal they could've sold. Let me walk you through exactly how it works, what the metals actually pay in 2026, and the one EPA rule that will fine you into the ground if you ignore it.

Why scrap metal is the most overlooked margin in junk removal

Most new haulers think of a load as a single transaction: customer pays, junk goes to the dump, you eat the tipping fee on the whole thing. Tipping fees in most US metros run $50 to $95 per ton in 2026. When you pull the metal out of that load before you weigh in at the transfer station, two things happen. You shrink your tipping bill, and you create a separate revenue line at the scrap yard.

I treat scrap as a side stream of the core business, not the business itself. If you're still figuring out the core, start with our guide on how to start a junk removal business and the real junk removal startup costs first. Scrap is the cherry on top, not the cake.

What metals actually pay (realistic 2026 ranges)

Scrap prices move daily with commodity markets, so treat every number here as an example range, not a quote. Call your local yard each morning before a big metal day. Prices also vary heavily by region and yard. That said, here's the lay of the land for a hauler in 2026.

The single most important concept: ferrous vs. non-ferrous. Ferrous metal (steel and iron, magnetic) pays the least. Non-ferrous (copper, brass, aluminum, not magnetic) pays multiples more per pound. A fridge magnet is your fastest sorting tool. If it sticks, it's steel. If it doesn't, slow down, because that piece is probably worth real money.

The water heater example

An old electric water heater weighs roughly 120 pounds. As light iron, that's maybe $5 to $14 at the yard. Not exciting. But many water heaters have a few feet of copper line and brass fittings attached. Cut those off, sort them separately, and that same unit might contribute $15 to $30 instead of $8. The difference is purely in the sorting.

The EPA refrigerant rule you cannot skip

This is the section that keeps you out of trouble, so read it twice. Refrigerators, freezers, window AC units, dehumidifiers, and some water coolers contain refrigerant. Under the federal Clean Air Act, that refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA Section 608 certified technician using certified recovery equipment before the appliance is scrapped or disposed of. You may never cut, puncture, or saw a refrigerant line to "let it out." Venting refrigerant is illegal, and per-violation penalties run into five figures.

You have two clean, legal paths as a hauler:

  • Pay a certified tech to recover the refrigerant. Many appliance recyclers and HVAC techs will pull refrigerant and tag the unit for $15 to $40 each. Once it's tagged as recovered, the steel carcass can go to the scrap yard.
  • Drop the whole appliance at a yard or recycler that handles recovery in-house. Some scrap yards accept sealed units and do recovery themselves. Many charge a small fee per unit ($10 to $25) rather than paying you for the steel. That fee is still cheaper than a transfer-station tip plus the legal risk.

Build the recovery cost into your pricing. When a customer wants a fridge removed, that job should be priced higher than a same-size non-refrigerant item, because you're absorbing a recovery fee. How to sort scrap so it actually pays

Yards pay top dollar for clean, separated, sorted metal. They pay bottom dollar (or refuse) mixed, dirty, "shred" loads. The money is in the sorting, and the sorting takes discipline more than skill.

  1. Set up a sort station at your shop or driveway. Four bins or marked areas: light iron, aluminum, copper/brass, and wire. Even a few totes work to start.
  2. Carry the magnet. Sticks = ferrous = steel pile. Doesn't stick = stop and look closer.
  3. Pull motors and compressors. Sealed electric motors and compressors usually have their own price tier higher than light iron. Don't let them disappear into the steel pile.
  4. Strip wire selectively. Bare bright copper pays the most. But stripping insulated wire by hand is slow. Strip only the thick stuff (heavy gauge, where the copper-to-jacket ratio is high). Thin wire is rarely worth the labor; sell it as insulated.
  5. Keep brass and copper apart. They look similar under grime but pay differently. A faucet is usually brass; a clean pipe is copper.

One safety note that's also a legal one: never burn wire to remove insulation. It's illegal under air-quality rules, it poisons you, and most yards will ban you for bringing in burnt wire. Strip it cold or sell it insulated.

Scrap yard basics for first-timers

Walking into a scrap yard the first time is intimidating. Here's the rundown so you look like you've done it before.

  • Bring a valid ID. Most states require yards to record your ID for theft-prevention reasons, especially on copper and catalytic converters. This is normal and legal.
  • Understand the weigh-in/weigh-out. You drive onto a scale full, dump your sorted piles, then weigh out empty. The difference is your sold weight, priced by material.
  • Ask for the day's posted prices. They change daily. Don't assume last week's number.
  • Sort before you arrive. If you dump a mixed pile, they grade the whole thing at the lowest metal's price. That's how a load of copper gets paid as steel.
  • Payment varies. Smaller yards pay cash up to a limit; larger ones issue checks or prepaid cards. Track every payout as taxable income.

Realistic extra income (not a get-rich pitch)

Let me be straight, because the internet is full of fantasy scrap numbers. For a typical owner-operator running a few jobs a day, scrap is a supplemental line, not a salary. These are example ranges from how the metal tends to fall out of normal loads, and your results will vary by region, yard prices, and the mix of jobs you book.

  • Per appliance-heavy job: an extra $10 to $40 in metal, after recovery fees.
  • A plumbing or HVAC remodel cleanout: occasionally $100 to $300 in copper and brass on a single job. These are the lottery tickets.
  • Steady monthly side stream: many part-time haulers report an example range of $150 to $600 a month from scrap, depending entirely on volume and metal mix.

The haulers who do best with scrap aren't chasing it. They're booking volume from the right sources, and the metal accumulates. Don't let scrap distract you from the boring fundamentals

I've watched new haulers get so excited about scrap that they skip the things that actually keep them in business. Don't.

You need real coverage before you're picking up anyone's appliances: general liability insurance (commonly $40 to $90 a month for a small hauler) plus commercial auto on your truck or van. Your personal auto policy will not cover a vehicle used for hauling, and a denied claim ends most one-truck operations. And to be clear, an LLC is a business structure, not a license or insurance. Forming an LLC does not make you legal to operate or covered when something goes wrong. Sort out the real requirements in junk removal license and insurance before you take your first paid job.

On the marketing side, get your free listings working. A Craigslist post in the labor/moving category costs $5 (it is not free), so write it well and renew it deliberately. On Facebook, Marketplace forbids listing services as items, so don't try to post your hauling service there as a "for sale" listing. Instead, set up a Facebook business Page and get active in local buy/sell and neighborhood groups where cleanout requests show up daily. Set up a Google Business Profile too, and ask happy customers to leave a review from their own phone or device after the job. More on all of this in how to get junk removal customers.

The bottom line on scrap

Scrap metal is a margin enhancer, not a business model. Pull the metal out of every load, sort it clean, respect the EPA refrigerant rule absolutely, and treat the scrap-yard check as a bonus on revenue you already earned hauling. Over a year, that discipline is the difference between a hauler who's just trading time for tipping fees and one who squeezes a second payday out of the same truck.

Get the fundamentals locked first, then let scrap quietly pad your numbers. That's how the operators who last actually run it.
