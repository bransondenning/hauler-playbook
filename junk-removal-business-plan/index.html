The One-Page Junk Removal Business Plan (Copy This Template)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Most junk removal business plans are written to impress a loan officer. Ours fits on one page, lives in a notes app, and actually tells us what to do on Monday. We run a junk removal company, and the plan that matters is the one you can read in two minutes and act on before lunch.

This is the version we wish we'd had on day one. It's seven sections: what you sell, who buys it, what it costs to start, how you get customers, what you're aiming to make, and the break-even math that tells you whether any of it works. Copy the headings into a doc, fill in your own numbers, and you've got a real plan. No 40-page appendix required.

Why a one-page plan beats a 40-page document

A bank document is built to forecast five years. A startup hauler needs to answer four questions: Can I price a job profitably? Where does the next call come from? What does it cost to turn the lights on? When do I stop losing money? Everything else is decoration.

The trap with long plans is that they make you feel productive while you avoid the hard part — picking up a phone and quoting a job. We've watched people spend three weeks on market-analysis slides and zero hours on customer outreach. The one-pager forces the opposite. Each section below ends with a blank you fill in. If you can't fill it in, that's your homework, not a reason to write another paragraph.

Section 1: Services and pricing

Decide what you haul and how you charge before anything else, because everything downstream — your trailer, your insurance, your ads — depends on it. For a startup, keep the service list short: residential cleanouts, single-item pickups (couches, mattresses, appliances), garage and basement clear-outs, small property turnovers, and light yard debris. Skip hazardous materials, full demolition, and anything needing a roll-off dumpster until you're established.

On pricing, we charge flat rate by trailer fraction, not by the hour. Hourly punishes you for being fast and scares customers who don't know how long a job takes. A flat-rate card lets you quote in seconds and protects your margin. Here's the kind of card we use as a starting point — adjust to your local market and dump fees:

The system that makes this fast is a photo quote: the customer texts you a photo of their pile, and you text back a flat price within the hour. No site visits, no "we'll call you to schedule an estimate." It compresses the sales cycle from days to minutes, and it's the single biggest reason we close more jobs than haulers who insist on in-person walkthroughs. Section 2: Target customers

Two customer types pay the bills, and they behave completely differently.

Homeowners (your volume)

Homeowners are one-off jobs: a garage that got out of hand, an old hot tub, a deceased parent's house. They find you through search, neighbors, and local groups. They care about trust and speed — they want someone who shows up when they said and doesn't surprise them on price. This is where most of your early revenue comes from, and where a clean review profile pays off.

Property managers (your recurring revenue)

Property managers and landlords are the accounts that change your business. When a tenant moves out and leaves junk behind, every day the unit sits full is a day they can't lease it — that's the vacancy-days pain you solve. A manager with 30 units calls you over and over, year after year, and never haggles because you're saving them money on turnover time.

The way in is an offer that rewards loyalty: we pitch managers a standing "20% off for life" rate in exchange for being their go-to hauler. It costs you margin on paper but the volume and predictability more than make up for it. One good property-manager relationship can outproduce a month of homeowner ads. Section 3: Startup costs

You can start a junk removal business for far less than people assume, because the truck-and-trailer route lets you avoid a six-figure rig. Here's a realistic 2026 startup range for someone using a pickup they already own plus a dump trailer:

  • Dump trailer (used, 6x12 or 7x14): $4,000–$8,000, or finance it for a few hundred a month
  • Hand tools, dollies, straps, tarps: $300–$600
  • LLC filing and business setup: $50–$300 depending on your state
  • General liability insurance: $40–$90/month is typical for a one-person operation
  • Initial dump/transfer station deposits and first loads: $200–$500
  • Marketing launch (see Section 4): $100–$400
  • Logo, business cards, magnetic signs: $100–$250

All in, a lean start lands somewhere around $5,000–$10,000 if you buy a trailer outright, or under $2,000 out of pocket if you finance the trailer. One legal note that trips people up: an LLC is not a license. In many cities and counties, hauling requires a local business or hauler permit on top of your LLC, and skipping it can mean fines. Check your municipality and carry that general liability policy — property managers will ask for a certificate of insurance before they give you a key. Section 4: Marketing channels

You don't need a big budget — you need three or four channels you actually work. Here's what produces calls for a new hauler, with the real costs and the rules that bite people who don't know them.

Google Business Profile

This is free and it's the highest-leverage thing you'll do. Set up a complete profile with your service area, photos of real jobs, and your phone number. Reviews matter, but get them the right way: after a job, text the customer your review link so they leave it from their own phone. Never collect reviews on a kiosk or your own device — that violates the rules and can get them filtered. And ignore anyone who tells you "20 reviews guarantees the map pack." There's no magic number; consistency and real customers are what move you up over time. Our Google Business Profile guide for haulers covers the setup step by step.

Craigslist

Craigslist still pulls calls, especially from price-shoppers and landlords. Post in the labor/move category — that's where hauling and labor services belong. Note that services posts cost $5 each in the US; Craigslist is not a free channel, so budget for it. A $5 post that lands one $275 job is one of the best returns you'll find.

Facebook

Facebook is powerful if you use it correctly. The play is a free business Page, not Marketplace. Marketplace prohibits listing a service as an item and will remove your posts. Instead, share your Page into local buy/sell and community groups, and answer the "ISO a hauler" or "anyone do junk removal?" posts that show up constantly. That direct, helpful presence in local groups beats any paid ad when you're starting out.

Nextdoor and word of mouth

Nextdoor reaches exactly your homeowner audience. Claim your business, be genuinely helpful, and let neighbors recommend you. Every clean job is a referral engine — a magnetic sign on your truck and a stack of cards left with happy customers does more over a year than most paid campaigns.

Section 5: Revenue targets

Set a target you can reverse-engineer into daily actions. In our experience, a solo operator running one trailer can typically complete 2–4 jobs a day once the phone is ringing, at an average ticket somewhere between $250 and $350. Those are example figures, not promises — your market, your close rate, and how hard you hustle decide where you land.

Run the math both directions. If your average job is $300 and you do 3 a day, that's $900 in revenue on a working day. Do that 4 days a week and you're looking at roughly $14,000–$15,000 in monthly revenue before expenses, in a good month with steady call volume. Many new haulers do far less than that in month one and ramp up; some never hit it because they don't market consistently. The honest range is wide, which is exactly why you track it weekly instead of guessing once.

Whatever target you pick, write it as jobs-per-week, not dollars-per-year. "I need 12 jobs this week at a $280 average" is something you can chase. "$150K a year" is not. If you want a grounded look at the upside and the failure modes, read is a junk removal business profitable.

Section 6: Break-even math

This is the section that keeps you in business, so don't skip it. Break-even is simple: add up what you owe every month no matter what, then figure out how many jobs cover it.

Say your fixed monthly costs look like this: trailer payment $300, insurance $70, phone and software $50, marketing $150 — that's $570 a month before you haul a single load. Now add variable costs per job: dump fees (often $40–$100 a load depending on weight and your transfer station), fuel, and the occasional helper. Call it $80 average variable cost on a $300 job, leaving roughly $220 of contribution per job.

Divide fixed costs by contribution per job: $570 ÷ $220 ≈ 3 jobs a month to break even. Everything past those three jobs is profit you split between your pay and reinvestment. That's the whole point of running the numbers — once you see break-even is three jobs, the business stops feeling like a gamble and starts feeling like arithmetic.

Watch two things that wreck the math. First, dump fees: heavy loads (concrete, dirt, shingles) cost far more to dump than light household junk, which is why your flat-rate card and your photo quotes need to account for weight, not just volume. Second, refrigerant appliances. Fridges, freezers, and AC units require EPA-certified refrigerant recovery before they can be scrapped or dumped — you cannot legally cut the lines yourself. Either charge extra to route them through a certified facility or decline them. Getting this wrong is a fine, not a shortcut.
