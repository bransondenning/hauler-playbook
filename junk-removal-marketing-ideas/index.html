Junk Removal Marketing Ideas: 15+ Tactics Ranked by Cost and Speed

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company, and the question we get most from people starting out isn't about trucks or dump fees. It's "how do I get the phone to ring?" The good news: you do not need a marketing budget to book your first 20 jobs. You need a few hours, a phone with a decent camera, and the discipline to do the boring stuff every day.

Below are 15+ marketing tactics ranked roughly by what they cost and how fast they pay off. Most are free or nearly free. Work them top to bottom. The free channels feed the phone this week; the slower ones build a pipeline that books itself by month three.

Free channels (do these this week)

1. Google Business Profile is your single highest-value asset

For a local service, your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the closest thing to a free customer faucet. Searches like "junk removal near me" pull from the map pack, and you cannot show up there without a verified profile. Set it up with your service-area cities, accurate hours, your real phone number, and the service categories that fit (Junk Removal Service, Garbage Collection Service). Add 10-15 photos of real jobs.

The lever that actually moves rankings is reviews. The right way to collect them: after a job, text the customer your review link so they leave it from their own phone. That keeps the review tied to a real account and a real location. Do not set up a review kiosk or collect reviews on your own device, and do not believe anyone who promises a magic number of reviews that "guarantees" the top spot. There is no threshold that locks in map-pack ranking. You compound your way up by consistently earning honest reviews. Aim for one or two a week. We go deeper on this in our junk removal Google Business Profile guide.

2. Craigslist services posts ($5 each, same-day leads)

Craigslist still books real jobs in most US metros. Post in the services > labor/move category. That is where hauling and labor ads belong. A services post costs $5 each in the US, so this is not a "free" channel, but at five bucks for same-day visibility it is one of the cheapest leads you can buy. Write a plain headline with your city and a starting price, list what you haul, drop in two before/after photos, and renew or repost when it ages off. One $5 post that lands a single garage cleanout pays for itself many times over.

3. Facebook: a business page, not a Marketplace listing

Here is a mistake that burns new haulers: do not list your service as an item on Facebook Marketplace. Marketplace prohibits listing a service as a product, and those posts get removed. The play that works is a free business Page plus participating in local buy/sell and community groups. Post your before/afters to the page, then share into neighborhood groups where it's allowed. Most importantly, monitor those groups for "ISO a hauler" or "anyone do junk removal?" posts and answer them fast with a friendly reply and your number. Those are warm, ready-to-book leads, and being first to respond wins most of them.

4. Nextdoor reaches the homeowners who actually pay

Nextdoor skews toward homeowners, which is exactly your buyer for garage, basement, and estate cleanouts. Claim a free business page, then watch for recommendation requests. When a neighbor asks for a hauler, a couple of genuine recommendations from past customers there carry more weight than any ad. Ask happy local customers to recommend you by name.

5. Before/after photo content is free fuel for everything

Every job is content. Snap a wide shot before you touch anything and a clean shot of the empty space after. That single pair of photos feeds your GBP, your Craigslist post, your Facebook page, and your Nextdoor profile. Nothing sells a cleanout like a packed garage going to bare concrete. Build the habit on job one. The kit we sell includes 30 ready-to-use social captions so you are not staring at a blank box every time you post.

Low-cost local marketing (under $25 to start)

6. Referral fees turn every customer into a salesperson

Pay $20-25 cash for any referral that turns into a paid job. Tell customers as you collect payment: "If you send a friend our way and we do the job, that's $25 in your pocket." It costs nothing until it works, and a referred lead closes far easier than a cold one because trust is already there.

7. Yard signs in front of active jobs

A corrugated yard sign runs a few dollars. Ask the homeowner if you can leave one at the curb during the job, neighbors see the truck and the sign together, and that is the most credible ad there is: proof you are working right there on their street. Pull it when you leave unless they're happy to keep it up.

8. Door hangers in the right neighborhoods

Door hangers cost roughly ten to twenty cents each printed. The trick is targeting. When you finish a job, hang 30-50 on the surrounding blocks. Same demographic, same need, and they just saw your truck. Older neighborhoods with aging owners and full garages convert best. This is a slow drip, not a blitz, but it pairs perfectly with a job you're already at.

Relationship channels (slower, but these become your bread and butter)

9. Property managers are recurring revenue

This is the single best pipeline in the business, and most new haulers ignore it because it takes a few weeks to land. Property managers and apartment complexes deal with tenant move-outs constantly, and every day a unit sits full of someone's abandoned junk is a vacancy day costing them rent. You solve a pain they feel in dollars.

Reach out with a simple pitch: fast turnarounds, photo quotes, reliable scheduling, and a standing discount, something like 20% off for life, in exchange for being their go-to. Once you're on a manager's list, you get called again and again without spending another dime on marketing. We walk through the whole approach in building property-manager cleanout accounts, and our kit includes the exact text, email, and cold-call scripts we use to open these doors.

10. Realtors need a fast, reliable hauler

Real estate agents have listings that won't show well until the seller's clutter is gone, and they have estate sales and pre-listing cleanouts on tight timelines. Be the hauler who answers the phone and shows up. Drop a few business cards at local offices, connect with agents on Facebook, and make it dead simple: they text you a photo, you text back a flat price within the hour. Agents refer the people who make them look good.

11. Photo-quote system: your fastest closing tool

Speed wins this business. Set up so a customer can text you a photo of their pile and get a flat price back within the hour. No scheduling an in-person estimate, no callback tag, just a fast number. That responsiveness is itself a marketing edge, because most of your competitors take a day to reply. It also pairs with flat-rate pricing so you can quote confidently from a photo. Paid and slow-burn options (when you have cash flow)

12. Truck or trailer wrap and lettering

Simple vinyl lettering with your name, service, and phone number runs a few hundred dollars; a full wrap can hit $2,000-2,500. It's a slow burn, not a lead faucet, but your rig is parked at every job and driving every road in your service area. At minimum, get clean lettering early. It makes you look established and turns commute miles into impressions.

13. Google Local Services Ads and Search Ads

Once you have a few reviews and some cash flow, paid Google ads put you at the top of the results you can't yet rank for organically. Local Services Ads charge per lead and show a Google Guaranteed badge. Start small, track cost per booked job, and only scale what's profitable. Do not run paid ads until your GBP and pricing are dialed, because you'll just pay to send leads to a weak profile.

14. A simple one-page website

You don't need a fancy site to start, but a clean one-page site with your services, service area, photos, price ranges, and a click-to-call button gives Google and customers somewhere to land. It also anchors your GBP. A basic site is cheap and removes friction for the buyer who wants to vet you before calling.

15. Niche down where the money is

Specializing makes your marketing sharper. Estate cleanouts, hoarding situations, and hot tub removals command premium pricing and have less competition. If you mention a niche in your posts and profile, you show up for those specific, higher-value searches. A few things that protect your marketing

Marketing brings calls; a couple of operational details keep them from blowing up on you. Carry general liability insurance, which typically runs $40-90 a month for a small hauler, because property managers and realtors will ask for proof of coverage before they hand you a key. And know that forming an LLC is not the same as a license. Many cities require a local hauler or business permit on top of it. Sort both before you lean hard on the relationship channels. We cover the details in junk removal license and insurance.

One more operational note that's really a marketing one: refrigerant appliances, fridges, freezers, and AC units, require EPA-certified refrigerant recovery before they can be scrapped. Never cut a refrigerant line. Handle these right, because the property managers and estate clients who send you the steady work expect a pro who knows the rules.

How to actually run this

Don't try all 15 at once. Here's the order we'd run if we were starting over today:

  1. Week 1: Set up and verify your GBP, post your first $5 Craigslist ad, build the Facebook page and Nextdoor page, and start texting review links after every job.
  2. Week 2-3: Add yard signs and door hangers around completed jobs, set up your photo-quote system, and start asking for referrals out loud.
  3. Week 3-6: Begin property-manager and realtor outreach. This is the slow burn that turns into your most reliable revenue.
  4. When cash flows: Add lettering or a wrap, then test paid Google ads, scaling only what books profitable jobs.

Marketing for junk removal isn't clever. It's showing up in the channels where people look, responding faster than the other guy, and stacking proof, photos and reviews and referrals, until the phone rings on its own. In our experience the first 20 jobs come almost entirely from the free list above.
