Junk Removal for Realtors and Home Sellers: How to Win the Agent Channel

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Ask any junk removal operator who's been at it for a couple of years where their best jobs come from, and a lot of them will say the same thing: a handful of real estate agents who text them before a listing goes live. Realtor work is the channel I wish I'd chased harder in year one. The jobs are clean, the addresses repeat, the decision-maker is motivated, and one good agent can send you a dozen cleanouts a year without you spending a dollar on ads.

This is the operator's playbook for junk removal for realtors and home sellers: what the work actually looks like, how to price it, how to hit the turnarounds agents need, and how to turn one job into a standing referral relationship.

Why the realtor channel beats cold leads

When a homeowner finds you on Google, you're a one-time transaction. When an agent finds you, you're potentially a vendor on every listing they take for the next five years. The math is what makes this worth your attention.

A working residential agent might close 15 to 30 transactions a year. Not all of those need a cleanout, but a meaningful slice do: the estate sale, the hoarder situation, the rental conversion, the seller who "ran out of time" before listing. If even a third of an agent's listings need junk hauled and you're the name in their phone, that's 5 to 10 jobs a year from one relationship. Land three or four agents like that and you've built a referral engine that runs without a marketing budget.

The other reason agents are gold: they're not price shoppers in the way a Craigslist tire-kicker is. An agent's real cost isn't your invoice; it's days on market. A house that shows full of junk sits, drops in price, and costs them their commission and their reputation. Clear it fast and they'll pay a fair rate and never blink. What pre-listing cleanouts actually involve

Pre-listing work falls into a few predictable buckets, and knowing them helps you quote on the spot:

  • Light declutter — seller is mostly moved out but left behind a garage of paint cans, a broken treadmill, and patio furniture. Half a trailer load, in and out in under an hour.
  • Full vacant cleanout — the house is empty of people but full of stuff. Furniture, mattresses, boxes, the contents of a basement. One to three trailer loads.
  • Estate cleanout — a parent passed, the family took the heirlooms, and the rest needs to disappear before the for-sale sign goes up. These are bigger, more emotional, and pay accordingly. If you want to specialize here, read our estate cleanout business breakdown.
  • Tenant trash-out — a landlord-seller had a tenant skip out and leave the place wrecked. Often paired with the property manager cleanout accounts side of the business.

The work itself isn't complicated. What separates the operator who gets repeat agent work from the one who doesn't is speed, communication, and not leaving a mess behind.

The appliance and hazard rules you can't skip

Cleanouts come with items you can't just toss. Refrigerators, freezers, window AC units, and dehumidifiers contain refrigerant, and the EPA requires certified recovery before disposal. You never cut the lines yourself. Either you hold EPA Section 608 certification and recover it properly, or you hand those units to a certified recycler. Paint, solvents, propane tanks, and chemicals are household hazardous waste and go to a designated facility, not the transfer station. Knowing these rules cold makes you look like a pro to an agent who's terrified of liability.

Pricing pre-listing jobs so agents keep calling

Most junk removal is priced by volume — what fraction of your truck or trailer the load fills, plus surcharges for heavy or special items. Pre-listing work follows the same model, but two things shift: jobs tend to be larger, and you're often quoting sight-unseen from agent photos.

One operator habit that pays off: give agents a simple "starting at" number for common scenarios so they can quote their sellers without waiting on you. "Garage cleanouts start at $150, full vacant homes start around $450" lets the agent sell the service for you.

Turnaround is the whole game

Agents don't remember your price. They remember whether you showed up when you said you would. The pre-listing window is brutally tight — photos are scheduled, the listing goes live Thursday, and the cleanout has to happen before the photographer arrives Wednesday morning.

To win on turnaround:

  1. Answer the phone. Agents call between showings. A missed call often goes to the next hauler on their list. If you can't answer live, return calls within the hour, every time.
  2. Quote from photos. Get comfortable giving a firm-ish range from six phone pictures. Agents work in texts and photos; meet them there.
  3. Hold same-week slots. Keep your schedule flexible enough to squeeze in a Tuesday cleanout for a Wednesday photo shoot. That flexibility is your competitive edge over bigger franchises booking a week out.
  4. Leave it broom-clean. Sweep the garage, knock down the cobwebs, haul the last bag. The agent walks in behind you and the house is ready to photograph.

Becoming an agent's go-to vendor

Getting the first job is the hard part. Keeping the relationship is mostly about being reliable and easy. Here's how operators build the standing referral that turns one agent into a pipeline.

Get in front of agents where they already are

  • Local brokerage offices. Drop off a stack of cards and a one-page rate sheet. Many offices have a Tuesday morning sales meeting — ask to do a two-minute intro.
  • Real estate Facebook groups. Most metros have agent-only or local-pro groups. Join, be helpful, and let people know you handle pre-listing cleanouts. Note: you'll use a Facebook Page for your business and post in local groups — Facebook Marketplace forbids listing services as items, so don't try to post cleanouts as "items for sale."
  • Title companies and stagers. They talk to the same agents you want. A stager who recommends you to clear a house before they style it is worth ten cold calls.
  • Craigslist. A services post in the labor/moving section costs $5 (it's not free) and still pulls homeowner and small-landlord work that often comes with an agent attached.

Make yourself the obvious referral

An agent refers you to their seller, which means their name is on the line if you screw up. Make that an easy, safe choice:

  • Show proof you're legit. Carry general liability insurance — typically $40 to $90 a month for a small hauler — plus commercial auto on your work vehicle. Be ready to email a certificate of insurance; agents and the brokerages they work for often ask. And be clear on the difference between entity and license: forming an LLC gives you a business entity and liability separation, but an LLC is not a license. Check your city and state for any local hauling or business-license requirements. The junk removal license and insurance guide walks through exactly what you need.
  • Be reachable and on time. The single biggest reason agents stop referring a vendor is no-shows and unanswered texts.
  • Send before-and-after photos. Text the agent a clean shot when you finish. They'll forward it to the seller, and it gives them confidence to call you next time.
  • Ask for the review on the customer's phone. When the seller is standing there happy, ask them to leave a Google Business Profile review from their own device. Reviews from the customer's own phone are how you build the profile that ranks. Never post reviews for people or from your own device.

Stay top of mind without being annoying

A quick text after each job — "house is cleared and broom-clean, photos are ready to go" — keeps you in the agent's recent contacts. A short note every quarter ("got a couple slots open this week if you have a listing coming up") is enough. You're not running a drip campaign; you're being a dependable phone number. Is the realtor channel worth building for a small operator?

If you're running this as a junk removal side hustle or scaling toward full-time, agent work is some of the highest-leverage marketing you can do because the cost to acquire each new job drops to nearly zero once the relationship exists. You did the work to earn the first referral; the next ten cost you a text message.

Run the numbers as an example, not a guarantee. Say three active agents send you six jobs each a year, and those jobs average $450. That's roughly $8,100 in annual revenue from relationships that cost you gas money and follow-through to maintain — on top of whatever your ads and Google traffic bring in. Land a couple of estate cleanouts in the mix and the figure climbs. Your actual results depend entirely on your market, your reliability, and how many agents you cultivate. Putting it together

The realtor channel rewards the boring stuff: answering the phone, quoting from photos, showing up Tuesday for a Wednesday shoot, leaving the place broom-clean, and texting a finished photo. Do that a dozen times and you stop being a hauler the agent found once and become the hauler the agent's whole office uses.
