Mattress Disposal and Removal: A Hauler's Guide to Rules, Fees, and Pricing

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

If you are starting a junk removal company and you want one job to cut your teeth on, take a mattress. It is heavy enough to feel like real work, awkward enough that the customer will never do it themselves, and small enough that you can do it solo with a hand truck and a pickup. I have hauled a few thousand of them. Mattress disposal and removal is the call I tell every new operator to chase first, because it teaches you pricing, disposal rules, and route math without the risk of a full house cleanout.

This is the operator's breakdown: what it actually costs you to get rid of a mattress, how the rules and fees change by region, where recycling fits in, and what to charge so you walk away with money instead of a sore back and a $0 ticket.

Why a mattress is the perfect first call

A mattress job is low-friction for one reason: the customer has already failed to solve it. They tried to fit it in a sedan. They asked the garbage company and got told no. They looked at the city bulk pickup schedule and it is three weeks out. By the time they call you, they are not price shopping. They want it gone today.

That changes the whole conversation. You are not competing on being cheapest. You are selling speed and "I do not have to think about this anymore." Single mattress pickups in 2026 typically run $75 to $150 depending on your market, with a queen set (mattress plus box spring) closer to $110 to $175. A two-person crew can knock one out in 15 to 20 minutes door to dump. The margin per minute is excellent compared to a multi-item load where you are sorting and Tetris-ing the trailer for an hour.

It is also a clean way to learn the trade. One predictable item, one known disposal path, one easy quote. Once you can price and route a mattress profitably, scaling up to couches, appliances, and full cleanouts is just more of the same skill. Disposal rules and fees by region

Here is where new haulers get burned. A mattress is not regular trash in a lot of places, and the disposal cost is not zero. You have to know your local rule before you quote, or your "profit" evaporates at the scale house.

Roughly speaking, the country splits three ways:

  • Mandatory recycling states. California, Connecticut, Rhode Island, and Oregon run statewide mattress recycling programs (the "Bye Bye Mattress" network). In these states many recyclers take mattresses for $0 to a few dollars because a fee is already baked into the retail purchase. This is the best-case scenario for you.
  • Per-item dump surcharge regions. Most of the rest of the country. Landfills and transfer stations accept mattresses but tack on a special handling fee, commonly $10 to $40 per mattress, on top of the by-weight or by-load tipping fee. A box spring usually counts as a second item.
  • Banned-from-landfill jurisdictions. A growing list of cities and counties will not landfill mattresses at all and route them to a recycler. You may have to drive farther and pay the recycler's gate fee.

The numbers below are realistic 2026 examples, not quotes. Call your own facility before you trust any of them, because rates move and every transfer station is a little different.

The takeaway: never quote a mattress before you know your disposal number. If your local transfer station charges $30 per mattress and you quoted the customer $75, you are working for $45 minus fuel and your time. That is the difference between a side hustle that pays and one that quietly loses money.

Recycling: good for the planet, sometimes good for the wallet

A mattress is roughly 80 to 90 percent recyclable by weight. Steel springs, foam, fiber, and wood all get separated and resold. In the mandatory-recycling states this is genuinely cheaper than landfilling. Outside those states it is usually a wash or slightly more expensive, but it gives you a marketing angle ("we recycle") and a cleaner story for commercial clients who care about diversion reporting.

One hard rule: recyclers will reject wet, moldy, or bed-bug-infested mattresses. If you pull a mattress out of a flooded basement or a unit with a known infestation, it is going to the landfill in a sealed bag, and you should price that job higher because of the contamination handling. Always eyeball the mattress before you commit to a recycling drop, or you will drive across town only to get turned away.

Pricing a mattress job so it actually pays

Forget guessing. Build the price from your costs up. Every mattress ticket has four pieces:

  1. Disposal cost. Your real gate fee per item. Know it cold.
  2. Labor. Your time door to dump. A solo single mattress might be 30 minutes round trip; a stair-carry from a third-floor walk-up is 45-plus and should cost more.
  3. Fuel and vehicle. Miles to the customer plus miles to the facility. A round trip to a far recycler eats your margin if you ignore it.
  4. Profit. The whole point. Aim for the disposal-plus-labor number to be a fraction of what you charge, not most of it.

A simple field rule that holds up: charge so that disposal and fuel together are no more than about a third of the ticket. If disposal is $30 and fuel is $10, your floor is roughly $120, not $75. Stairs, long carries, and disassembly (some adjustable bases have to come apart) are add-ons, not freebies. For the full system on building a price card you can hand a customer with confidence, the junk removal pricing guide lays it out item by item.

Stacking matters too. One mattress at $100 is fine. Three mattresses from an apartment turnover at $80 each, hauled in one trip with one dump run, is a much better hour because your fixed costs (drive time, fuel, the trip to the facility) get spread across three tickets. That is why volume from repeat sources beats one-off calls. Property turnovers are the cleanest volume there is.

The startup math behind a mattress route

You do not need a $60,000 box truck to haul mattresses. A pickup with a tie-down strap and a furniture dolly will move a mattress all day. Plenty of operators start with a trailer they already own. If you are weighing the rig question, junk removal truck vs trailer compares the real costs, and junk removal business startup costs breaks down what you actually need to spend before your first job versus what can wait.

Keep the launch lean. A pickup or small dump trailer, ratchet straps, gloves, a hand truck, contractor bags for the messy ones, and a phone. That is a working setup. The mistake is over-buying gear before you have proven you can book and close jobs.

Insurance and the legal basics, said straight

Two things new haulers get wrong. First, forming an LLC is not a license. An LLC is a liability structure. Whether you need a local business license, a hauler's permit, or anything else depends on your city and county, and you should check before you advertise.

Second, carry insurance before you carry other people's junk. General liability for a small junk removal operation typically runs about $40 to $90 a month, and you will also want commercial auto coverage on the vehicle you haul with, because a personal auto policy can deny a claim the moment it learns you were working. Property managers and apartment complexes will ask for a certificate of insurance before they let you on the property, so this pays for itself the day you land your first account. One safety note that applies the moment you scale up

Mattresses are simple. The trouble starts when the same customer says "while you're here, can you take the old fridge?" Refrigerators, freezers, window AC units, and dehumidifiers contain refrigerant, and that refrigerant has to be recovered by an EPA-certified technician before the unit is scrapped. You never cut or puncture the lines yourself. Either subcontract the recovery, build a relationship with a scrapper who handles it, or charge enough to cover proper disposal. It is a federal rule, not a suggestion, and it is exactly the kind of thing that turns an easy mattress call into a headache if you are not ready for it.

Getting mattress calls in the first place

The demand is constant because people replace mattresses and never plan for the old one. Here is where the leads come from without spending much:

  • Google Business Profile. Free, and it is the single highest-intent channel for "mattress removal near me." Fill it out completely, post photos of real jobs, and ask every happy customer to leave a review from their own phone after the job. Reviews from the customer's own device are what keep the profile healthy and ranking.
  • Craigslist. A services post costs $5 (it is not free anymore) and runs in the labor / moving / hauling category. Cheap, and the people browsing there are ready to hire.
  • Facebook. You cannot list a service as an item on Marketplace, so do not try. Run a simple Facebook Page for your business and be active in local buy-sell-trade and neighborhood groups where people constantly ask "who hauls junk?"
  • Property managers. Apartment turnovers generate mattresses on a schedule. One account can be a mattress (and couch, and full unit) every single week.

Bottom line

Mattress disposal and removal is the ideal first call because the customer is already sold, the job is fast, and the only thing standing between you and profit is knowing your local disposal rule and pricing above it. Learn your gate fee. Quote so disposal and fuel stay under a third of the ticket. Recycle where it is cheaper or where it wins you commercial work. Then stack jobs into single trips and let the route math do the heavy lifting. Do that consistently and the mattress that everyone else dreads becomes the easiest money on your schedule.
