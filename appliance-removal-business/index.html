Appliance Removal and Haul Away: The Pricing and Profit Playbook

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Most haulers chase the big cleanout job and ignore the easy money sitting in someone's garage: a dead washer, a fridge nobody wants to load, a stove the new owner is replacing. Appliance removal and haul away is the most underrated entry point in this business. The jobs are fast, the phone rings often, and half the time that single-item pickup turns into a driveway full of work you didn't know was there.

This is how we price appliance jobs, stay legal on the refrigerant rule, squeeze a little scrap value out of the metal, and turn a $90 fridge pickup into a $600 garage cleanout. These are example numbers from a real operating market in 2026 - your area will vary, and nothing here is guaranteed income.

Why appliances are the perfect lead-in service

An appliance pickup is a low-commitment ask. A homeowner who would never call about a "cleanout" will absolutely call to get a broken dryer out of the basement. That low barrier is the whole point. You show up, you're professional, you're already standing in their house - and now you're the junk person they think of when the garage, the storage unit, or grandma's house comes up.

The numbers back it up. In our shop, roughly one in four single-item appliance calls produces an upsell or a callback within 60 days. The appliance job pays for the truck roll; the upsell is where the margin lives. If you're thinking about how to start a junk removal business, appliances are the cheapest, fastest way to get reps and reviews early.

What to charge for appliance removal and haul away

Appliances are priced by the item, not by volume, because they're heavy, awkward, and predictable. Here's a realistic 2026 price card for a typical US market. Adjust for your dump fees and labor costs.

ApplianceTypical price (example)Notes
Microwave / small unit$45 - $75Often bundled with a bigger item
Washer or dryer$75 - $120Two-person lift; basement adds $25 - $40
Dishwasher$70 - $110Disconnect if customer hasn't
Stove / range$75 - $115Gas line must be capped by customer
Refrigerator / freezer$95 - $160Refrigerant handling - see below
Water heater$90 - $140Heavy; drain first

Three pricing rules that keep you profitable:

  • Set a minimum service charge. Ours is $95. It's never worth rolling a truck for a $45 microwave alone. The minimum protects your time and your fuel.
  • Charge for stairs and distance. A fridge from a finished basement up a tight stairwell is a different job than one in an open garage. Add $25 - $50 for a hard carry.
  • Bundle the second item. "I'll take the washer for $95, and since I'm here, the dryer too for $60 more." Customers say yes constantly. Your second item is almost pure margin because the truck is already there.

The refrigerant rule you cannot ignore

Refrigerators, freezers, window AC units, and dehumidifiers contain refrigerant. Under the federal Clean Air Act, that refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA Section 608 certified technician before the appliance is disposed of or scrapped. This is not optional, and the fines for venting refrigerant are real and steep.

The hard rule for your crew: never cut, crimp, or puncture a refrigerant line. Venting refrigerant to the atmosphere is illegal, and "I didn't know" is not a defense.

You have two clean ways to handle this:

  1. Get certified. EPA 608 certification is inexpensive and lets you recover refrigerant in-house with the right recovery machine. This is the path if you're doing volume.
  2. Partner out. Take refrigerant units to a scrap yard or appliance recycler that's certified to recover and document it. Many will take the unit and handle the recovery. Build that relationship before you need it.

Either way, your customer doesn't see the headache. They see you load the fridge and drive off. Behind the scenes, the refrigerant is handled by someone licensed to do it. Bake the extra step into your fridge pricing - that's why a refrigerator costs more to haul than a dryer.

Scrap value: real money, but know what it is

Appliances are mostly steel, and steel has a price. Most appliances run 60 - 120 pounds of recoverable metal. At ferrous scrap rates that bounce around but often sit near $0.06 - $0.12 per pound in 2026, a typical appliance might net $6 - $14 at the yard. Copper and aluminum from motors and compressors are worth more per pound, but it's a small amount.

Here's the honest framing: scrap is a tip, not a salary. The real money is the haul-away fee you charged the customer. But scrap does two useful things - it can offset or even cover your dump fees, and many scrap yards take appliances free or for a small payout instead of charging you a disposal fee. On a busy week, that's the difference between paying $40 in dump tickets and breaking even on disposal entirely.

A few yard realities:

  • Many yards require refrigerant units to come with recovery documentation, or they'll deduct a fee.
  • Strip nothing on the customer's property. Load whole, sort at the yard.
  • Track your scrap income - it's taxable, and it's a real line item once you're running volume.

How to get the phone ringing for appliance jobs

Appliance keywords are some of the easiest local searches to rank and advertise for because the intent is dead simple - someone wants a thing gone. Here's where the cheap, real leads come from:

  • Google Business Profile. This is your number one free lead source. Fully fill it out, list "appliance removal" as a service, and ask every happy customer for a review. Important: the customer leaves the review from their own phone or device - you can't post it for them. A profile with 30 real reviews outranks an empty one every time.
  • Craigslist. Post in the services section under labor / moving. Craigslist services posts cost $5 each - they are not free - but $5 for a post that can produce a $95 job is a strong return. Refresh weekly.
  • Facebook, done right. You cannot list a service as an "item for sale" on Facebook Marketplace - it's against their rules and your post gets pulled. Instead, run a Facebook business Page and post in local buy/sell/community groups where service mentions are allowed. "Same-day appliance haul-away, $95, licensed and insured" in the right local group works.
  • Yard signs and door hangers. When you finish a job, the neighbors saw your truck. A sign in the yard for the day costs nothing.

Turning single items into cleanouts

This is the part most haulers miss. The appliance call is a foot in the door - literally. Once you're inside, you're looking at the whole space. The move is simple: do a great job on the appliance, then ask one question.

"While I've got the truck here - is there anything else you've been meaning to get rid of?"

That sentence has paid for more of my trucks than any ad. A dead fridge becomes a garage cleanout. A washer pickup becomes "actually, my dad passed and we need the whole house emptied." Appliance jobs are how you find estate cleanout work and recurring property manager cleanout accounts - because property managers always have appliances to swap between tenants, and the hauler who handles the fridge fast becomes the hauler who handles the move-out.

The legal and insurance basics

You're carrying heavy things out of people's homes and driving them around. Get covered before your first paid job:

  • General liability insurance. Expect roughly $40 - $90 per month for a small hauling operation. It covers property damage - the dinged doorframe, the scratched floor - which happens more with appliances than anything else.
  • Commercial auto insurance. Personal auto policies typically exclude business use. If you're hauling for money, you need a commercial auto policy on the truck or van.
  • Know what an LLC is and isn't. An LLC is a business structure for liability and taxes. It is not a license or a permit, and it doesn't replace insurance. You may also need a local business license depending on your city.

Is the appliance angle actually worth it?

Run the math on a clean week. Say you do twelve appliance jobs at an average ticket of $110 - that's $1,320. Subtract maybe $120 in dump fees (partly offset by scrap), $80 in fuel, and your insurance share, and you're keeping the large majority of that as gross profit, before your own labor. Now add the two cleanout upsells those appliance jobs produced at $450 each, and the week looks very different.

That's the model: appliances are the reliable base, cleanouts are the upside. These are example numbers, not a promise - your dump fees, drive times, and close rate decide the real figure.
