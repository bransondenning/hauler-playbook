Hot Tub Removal Service Business: The $350-600 Add-On Every Junk Hauler Should Sell

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company, and if you asked me which single line item moves the needle most on a slow week, it's not the garage cleanout or the mattress haul. It's the old hot tub rotting in someone's backyard. A hot tub removal job typically bills $350 to $600, often takes one to two hours of actual labor, and the customer is usually thrilled to pay because they have no idea how to get the thing out themselves. That spread between what you charge and what it costs you to do is why we treat hot tub removal as one of the most profitable jobs on the board.

This is a practical walkthrough of how the hot tub removal service business actually works: why it commands premium pricing, the tools you need, the step-by-step removal process, the safety you cannot skip, where the carcass goes, and how to market the service specifically so the calls come in.

Why hot tub removal commands premium pricing

A hot tub is not a couch. The customer can't drag it to the curb, a regular trash service won't touch it, and most people genuinely don't know it has to be cut apart to leave the yard. That combination of can't-do-it-myself plus don't-know-who-to-call is exactly what lets you charge a premium. You're not competing with the customer's nephew and a borrowed truck.

Here's the cost reality on a typical job in 2026:

  • Labor: 1-2 hours, usually a two-person crew. Demo and load is fast once the panels are off.
  • Disposal: A demolished hot tub fits in roughly a quarter to a half of a standard dump trailer. Dump/landfill fees vary by region but commonly run $40-90 for that volume.
  • Consumables: A handful of reciprocating saw blades. Acrylic and fiberglass shells eat blades, so budget a few dollars per job.

So on a $450 job, your hard costs are often well under $150, and most of that is your own time. In our experience the margin on hot tub removal beats almost every other category we run except maybe single-item appliance pickups. That's why we never quote it as a generic "junk" rate — it's a specialty service, and it's priced like one.

How to set your price

We price hot tubs on three variables: size, access, and condition. A standard 6-person tub sitting on an open concrete pad next to a wide gate is your floor price. Add to it when any of these are true:

  • Tough access: stairs, a deck it's recessed into, a narrow side yard, or a backyard you have to haul pieces 100+ feet to reach the trailer. This is where most amateurs underbid and lose money.
  • Built-in or in-deck installs: the tub is framed into a deck and you have to demo decking to free it.
  • Swim spas or large shells: these are heavier, longer, and sometimes need more cutting.
  • Crane or hoist situations: rare, but rooftop or steep-lot tubs are a different animal — quote those separately or pass.

The tools you actually need

The beauty of hot tub removal is that the tool list is short and cheap. You do not need heavy equipment for a standard residential job.

  • Reciprocating saw (sawzall): the workhorse. A corded model or two good battery platforms. This cuts the shell, the cabinet framing, and the foam in minutes.
  • Demolition blades: bring a dozen. Acrylic, fiberglass, and the embedded metal frame dull blades fast. Wood/metal combo blades handle most of it.
  • Cordless drill/impact driver: for backing out cabinet screws before you cut, which is faster and cleaner than sawing everything.
  • Pry bar and utility knife: for panels, foam, and the vinyl/wood skirt.
  • Submersible utility pump or wet/dry vac: for draining residual water the customer didn't get out.
  • PPE: cut-resistant gloves, safety glasses, ear protection, and a dust mask. Cutting fiberglass and foam throws nasty particulate.
  • Furniture dolly and ratchet straps: for moving cut sections to the trailer.

That's a kit most haulers already own. The saw and blades are the only hot-tub-specific spend, and a quality demo setup pays for itself on the first job. The step-by-step hot tub removal process

Here's the sequence we run on a standard residential tub. Do it in order — skipping steps is how people get hurt or flood a yard.

  1. Disconnect the power first. Always. Hot tubs are hardwired to a 240-volt circuit through a GFCI disconnect box, or plugged into a dedicated 120V circuit. Shut off the breaker at the panel, confirm it's dead, and if it's hardwired, have the homeowner's electrician disconnect it — or do it only if you are genuinely qualified. Never start cutting on an energized tub. This is the non-negotiable safety step.
  2. Drain it. Most tubs have a drain valve at the base; open it and let gravity work, or speed it up with a submersible pump. A full tub holds 300-500 gallons, so plan where that water goes — away from foundations and toward a drain or the street, per local rules. Vacuum out the last few inches.
  3. Pull the side panels and skirt. Unscrew or pry off the cabinet panels to expose the plumbing, pumps, and the foam-packed cavity. Now you can see what you're cutting into.
  4. Cut the shell into manageable sections. With the sawzall, section the acrylic/fiberglass shell into pieces two people can carry. We typically quarter a standard tub. Cut through the foam insulation as you go.
  5. Separate the frame and equipment. The wood or composite frame, the pump motor, and any metal come apart easily once the shell is open. Metal and motors can sometimes be scrapped for a few extra dollars.
  6. Load and haul out. Dolly the sections to the trailer. A clean two-person crew clears a standard tub in well under two hours start to finish.
  7. Sweep the pad. Leave the spot cleaner than you found it. Foam crumbs everywhere is how you lose the five-star review. Which, by the way, is worth more than the job.

Safety, disposal, and the legal side

Electrical is the real risk

The one thing that can seriously hurt you on this job is the 240V hookup. Treat every tub as live until you've personally confirmed the breaker is off and the disconnect is open. If the wiring looks sketchy or you're not comfortable, the customer pays for a licensed electrician to disconnect before you start. That conversation costs you nothing and protects everyone.

Where the hot tub goes

A demolished hot tub is mostly landfill material — acrylic, fiberglass, foam, and treated wood. It goes to your local transfer station or C&D (construction and demolition) landfill. Call ahead the first time to confirm they take it and what the fee is. The pump motor and any metal framing can go to a scrap yard if you want to recover a few dollars, but don't build your price around scrap value.

One disposal note that trips up newer haulers: if a job ever includes a refrigerator, freezer, or AC unit alongside the tub, those contain refrigerant and legally require EPA-certified recovery before they can be scrapped or landfilled. Never cut a refrigerant line. That's a separate certification and a separate handling process — keep it out of your hot tub workflow entirely.

License, insurance, and your business setup

Before you advertise demolition-style work, get your house in order. Carry general liability insurance — for a small hauler that typically runs $40-90 a month — because you're cutting power equipment in someone's backyard and things happen. And understand that forming an LLC is not a license: in plenty of cities, hauling and debris transport require a local permit on top of your business entity. Check your municipality. How to market hot tub removal specifically

Generic "we haul junk" ads bury this service. Hot tub removal is a search-intent job — when someone's tub dies, they go straight to their phone and type exactly what they want. Your marketing needs to match that intent.

  • Make it a named service on your site and Google Business Profile. Don't hide it under "other items." A dedicated "Hot Tub Removal" service line — with a price range and a few before/after photos — is what gets you into the local map results when someone searches it. There's no magic review count that guarantees the map pack, but steady, real reviews help. The right way to get them: text the customer your Google review link from their own phone after the job. Never collect reviews on your own device or a kiosk — that gets them filtered. More on this in our Google Business Profile setup guide.
  • Run a Craigslist services post. Post under the labor/move category. Craigslist services posts cost $5 each in the US — it's not a free channel, but $5 to land one $450 job is the best ad spend you'll find. Title it plainly: "Hot Tub Removal & Disposal — Same Week."
  • Use a free Facebook business Page, not Marketplace. Facebook Marketplace prohibits listing a service as an item — it'll get removed. The play that works is a free business Page, then sharing your hot tub removal post into local buy/sell and community groups, and answering "ISO a hauler" or "who removes hot tubs?" posts when they pop up. Those questions appear constantly and the first credible reply usually wins the job.
  • Answer it on Nextdoor. Neighbors ask for hot tub haulers regularly. A polite, specific reply with your price range and a photo of past work converts well in higher-income neighborhoods where the tubs are.
  • Use a photo-quote system. Tell people: text us a photo of your hot tub and where it sits, and we'll text back a flat price within the hour. It removes friction, lets you price access correctly before you commit, and customers love the speed. This same photo-to-flat-price system is how we quote most of our work.

Don't sleep on property managers

Rental properties and foreclosures come with dead hot tubs more often than you'd think, and the property managers handling those vacancies are repeat buyers who don't haggle the way homeowners do. Land a few of these accounts and hot tub removal becomes a recurring line, not a one-off. We break down that outreach in our guide to property manager cleanout accounts.

Is it worth adding? Run the numbers

Let's be honest about what this realistically looks like, framed as a typical example, not a promise. Say you land two hot tub jobs a week at an average of $425. That's roughly $850 a week in revenue from a service that adds almost nothing to your overhead — you already own the trailer and most of the tools. After disposal and blades, the margin on those two jobs alone can cover a meaningful chunk of your monthly insurance and fuel. Stack that on top of your regular cleanouts and the math gets attractive fast.

It won't replace your whole income by itself, and volume depends entirely on your market and how aggressively you market it — but as a high-margin add-on bolted onto an existing operation, few services compete.
