Junk Removal Insurance Cost: 2026 Premiums by Policy Type

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Every week someone in a junk removal Facebook group asks the same thing: "How much is insurance going to cost me before I can even haul my first load?" The honest answer is that junk removal insurance cost usually lands somewhere between $50 and $250 a month when you stack the policies you actually need together. The spread is wide because "insurance" isn't one bill. It's three or four separate policies, and most new operators either overpay for coverage they don't need or skip the one policy that gets them sued out of business.

I run an operating junk removal company, and this is the breakdown I wish someone had handed me on day one. Real policy types, real 2026 premium ranges, and what you can skip until the revenue justifies it.

The four policies that matter (and which you actually need)

Before we talk numbers, understand that you're not buying "junk removal insurance." You're assembling a stack. Here's the short version of who needs what:

  • General liability (GL) — non-negotiable. Get this before your first job.
  • Commercial auto — required if you drive a truck or tow a trailer for business. Your personal policy will deny a work claim.
  • Inland marine / cargo — covers the stuff you're hauling and your gear. Cheap, often skipped, occasionally a lifesaver.
  • Workers comp — only when you hire employees. Not needed for a true solo operator in most states.

One thing to clear up early because it confuses everyone: an LLC is not a license, and it is not insurance. Forming an LLC separates your personal assets from the business on paper, but it does nothing if a couch slides off your trailer onto someone's Tesla. You need the actual liability policy. For the full legal-vs-coverage picture, see our guide on junk removal license and insurance.

General liability: $40–$90/month

This is the foundation. General liability covers third-party bodily injury and property damage — the dolly that scrapes a hardwood floor, the dropped armoire that dents a garage door, the customer who trips over a box you set down. In 2026, a solo junk hauler with a $1M-per-occurrence / $2M-aggregate GL policy typically pays $40 to $90 per month, or roughly $500 to $1,100 a year.

What moves your premium:

  • Revenue. A $60K/year operation pays less than a $250K one. Carriers quote off projected gross.
  • Payroll. Crew on the books raises GL too, separate from workers comp.
  • State. Litigious, high-cost states (FL, CA, NY, NJ) run higher than the Midwest.
  • Claims history. Clean record keeps you at the floor of the range.

Most property managers and commercial accounts will require proof of GL before they hand you a key — usually a $1M policy listing them as an additional insured (a $0–$25 add-on per certificate). That single requirement is why GL pays for itself the moment you chase the kind of recurring work covered in our property manager cleanout accounts guide.

Commercial auto: $120–$300/month

Here's the one people try to dodge, and it's a mistake. If you haul junk in a vehicle, your personal auto insurer can — and will — deny the claim the second they learn you were working. A denied claim on a totaled truck is how solo operators go bankrupt in their first year.

Commercial auto is the single most expensive line in your stack. In 2026, expect $120 to $300 per month ($1,400–$3,600/year) for a single truck, depending on vehicle value, driving record, garaging location, and coverage limits. Box trucks and dump trucks sit at the top; an older pickup at the bottom.

A few money-savers worth knowing:

  • If you're towing a trailer behind a personal truck, some carriers let you add a commercial endorsement to a personal policy for less than a full commercial line — confirm in writing that hauling for hire is covered.
  • Higher deductibles ($1,000–$2,500) cut the monthly bill meaningfully.
  • Your trailer itself is usually covered under the auto policy for liability, but the cargo on it is not — that's inland marine, below.

Inland marine / cargo: $20–$60/month

This is the quiet, cheap policy that most new haulers skip and occasionally regret. Inland marine covers property in transit and your business equipment — the customer's belongings while they're on your trailer, plus your dollies, straps, appliance hand truck, and tools if they're stolen out of your vehicle overnight.

For a solo operator, cargo/inland marine runs about $20 to $60 per month for $10K–$25K of coverage. It's the kind of policy you don't think you need until a strap fails on the highway, or someone breaks into your truck and walks off with $1,500 of gear. At $30 a month, it's cheap insurance against a bad week.

Workers comp: only when you hire

If you're a true solo operator with no employees, you generally do not need workers comp — and in most states you can't even buy a meaningful policy on just yourself. The moment you put a helper on payroll, that changes fast.

Junk removal is heavy, physical work, so comp class codes price accordingly. Budget roughly $3 to $8 per $100 of payroll in 2026, varying heavily by state. On a $40,000/year crew member, that's around $1,200–$3,200 a year. It's required by law in nearly every state once you have W-2 employees, and skipping it invites fines that dwarf the premium. Pay a 1099 contractor instead and you sidestep comp, but misclassifying a real employee as a contractor is its own expensive problem — talk to a local accountant before you decide.

How insurance fits into your pricing and your profit

Insurance is a fixed monthly cost, which means the more jobs you run, the less it costs you per haul. At $250/month in total premiums and 20 jobs a month, that's about $12.50 of insurance baked into each job — pennies against a typical $200–$500 ticket. Build it into your rates from the start so it never feels like a surprise; our junk removal pricing guide shows exactly how to load fixed costs into a price card.

To put the number in context with realistic example revenue: a part-time solo operator running 3–4 jobs a week at an average $250 ticket might gross around $3,000–$4,000 a month — these are example ranges, not guarantees, and your market, marketing, and effort decide where you actually land. Against that, a $200 insurance bill is a small, fully deductible line item. Insurance doesn't kill your margin; one uninsured accident does.

A few field notes that save you money and headaches

Two operational realities that intersect with your coverage and your reputation:

  • Refrigerant appliances are a regulated hazard, not a free score. Fridges, freezers, AC units and dehumidifiers contain refrigerant that is illegal to vent. Never cut the lines yourself — federal EPA rules require certified recovery by a technician before disposal. Most transfer stations charge a small fee to take them properly. Mishandling this isn't just a fine risk; it's exactly the kind of negligence that can void a claim.
  • Get your insurance certificate squared away before you market. Commercial accounts ask for it on day one. For where to actually find the work once you're covered, see how to get junk removal customers.

On the marketing side, a quick accuracy check most new haulers get wrong: posting a junk removal service on Craigslist costs $5 per post (it's not free) — list it under labor/move. And do not try to list your service as an item on Facebook Marketplace; Marketplace forbids service listings posing as items. Instead, run a proper Facebook business Page and post in local buy/sell and community groups. For reviews, the cleanest way to build your Google Business Profile is to hand the customer the review link and let them leave it from their own device — Google flags reviews left on your phone.

The bottom line on junk removal insurance cost

Plan on $60–$150/month if you start lean as a solo operator towing a trailer, and $180–$450/month for a fully-covered single-truck operation with commercial auto. The only truly non-negotiable policy is general liability — get it before your first job. Add commercial auto the moment you drive for work, layer in cargo coverage because it's cheap, and bring on workers comp only when you put someone on payroll.
