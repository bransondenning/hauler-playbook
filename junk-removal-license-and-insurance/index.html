Junk Removal Business License and Insurance: What You Actually Need in 2026

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Getting legal is the least exciting part of starting a junk removal business, and it is also the part that wins the jobs that matter. Homeowners hire whoever answers the phone first. Property managers, realtors, and contractors - the clients who hand you repeat work month after month - will not let you on a property without proof of insurance. We run a junk removal company, and our certificate of insurance has closed more commercial accounts than any ad we have ever paid for.

This guide covers the whole junk removal business license and insurance stack for a US operation in 2026: business structure (LLC vs. sole proprietorship), your EIN, general liability insurance, city and county licenses, waste hauler rules, and the short list of items you should refuse to load. If you are still deciding whether this business is for you, start with our full guide on how to start a junk removal business, then come back. For everyone else: $300-$700 up front, $50-$150 a month ongoing, and one focused afternoon of paperwork.

LLC vs. sole proprietorship

A sole proprietorship is what you have by default the moment you accept money for hauling a couch. It costs nothing, requires no paperwork, and gets taxed on Schedule C with your personal return. The problem is the nature of this work: junk removal is a liability business. You carry 300-pound sleeper sofas down stairwells past drywall and parked cars. A trailer can come unhitched. A helper can drop a treadmill on someone's foot. As a sole proprietor, you and the business are the same legal person, so a judgment that exceeds your insurance limits comes out of your personal assets - your truck, your savings, your house.

An LLC puts a legal wall between business liabilities and personal assets. It also does three practical things people underrate: it lets you open a business bank account, which keeps your bookkeeping sane and your liability shield intact; it looks like a real company on a property-management vendor application; and it gives you a brand to stack reviews under.

What the LLC actually costs

State filing fees in 2026 range from $35 in Montana to $500 in Massachusetts, with most states in the $50-$150 band, and most process online filings within a week. Watch the recurring fees too: many states charge a $20-$100 annual report fee, and California charges every LLC an $800-per-year franchise tax - the one state where starting out as a well-insured sole prop is understandable.

Taxes are a wash at the start. A single-member LLC is a disregarded entity - the IRS taxes it exactly like a sole proprietorship by default. The S-corp election that saves real money rarely makes sense below roughly $50,000-$60,000 a year in net profit; talk to an accountant then, not now.

When a sole prop is fine

If you are only testing the waters with weekend loads, a sole proprietorship plus a real general liability policy is a defensible start - insurance, not entity structure, is what protects you on day one. Our line in the sand: file the LLC before you sign with a commercial client or hire a helper.

Get your EIN - free and ten minutes

An EIN (Employer Identification Number) is the business equivalent of a Social Security number, and it is free at irs.gov in about ten minutes - anyone charging $50 for one is reselling a free government form. You need it to open the business bank account, and again the first time a property manager sends a W-9 before paying your invoice. Even if you stay a sole proprietor, get one anyway: it keeps your SSN off every W-9 you hand out.

Insurance: the part that actually wins you work

General liability: the $1M policy

General liability is the policy this industry runs on. The standard ask - the number commercial clients write into vendor requirements - is $1 million per occurrence and $2 million aggregate. In 2026, a one-person junk removal operation typically pays $40-$90 per month for that coverage from online-first carriers like Next Insurance, Thimble, or Hiscox. Quotes take fifteen minutes; get all three, because we have seen the spread hit 40% for identical coverage. One warning: classify yourself honestly as junk removal, hauling, or debris removal - calling it "landscaping" to shave $15 a month is how claims get denied.

Know what the policy does and does not do. General liability covers damage to other people's property and injuries to people who are not your employees: the dresser corner through the drywall, the gouged hardwood, the bystander who trips over your dolly. It does not cover your truck (that is auto insurance), your tools, your own injuries, or your helper's. And read this twice: many policies exclude damage to the item you are actually carrying under the "care, custody, and control" exclusion - ask the carrier how yours handles it before you agree to move grandma's piano.

The certificate of insurance is a sales tool

A certificate of insurance (COI) is the one-page proof that your policy exists, and in this business it is a closing tool. Next and Thimble generate COIs instantly from their apps and will add a property manager as an "additional insured" at no charge. When a property manager asks five haulers for a COI and only two can produce one the same day, the bid list just got shorter. That single page is the entry ticket to recurring cleanout work - we walk through landing those accounts in our property manager cleanout guide.

Commercial auto: the one people skip and regret

Your personal auto policy almost certainly excludes business use. If you rear-end someone while hauling a paid load and the insurer figures out it was commercial work - and they will, because the police report mentions a trailer full of furniture - the claim can be denied outright. Commercial auto for a single pickup truck typically runs $120-$300 per month in 2026 depending on your state, driving record, and radius. Your trailer's liability generally follows the tow vehicle's policy, but physical damage coverage on the trailer itself has to be added separately - worth doing once you own a $5,000 dump trailer.

Helpers and workers' comp

The first time you pay a helper, look up your state's workers' compensation threshold. Most states require coverage at one employee; a few, like Texas, make it optional. "I paid him cash, he is not really an employee" is not a defense - if a helper herniates a disc on a hide-a-bed, you can be personally on the hook for the medical bills. Budget roughly $2,000-$4,000 a year for a full-time mover, or use a pay-as-you-go policy billed against actual payroll so a part-timer costs a fraction of that.

City and county business licenses

There is no federal junk removal license, and most states do not license general non-hazardous hauling either. The licensing that actually applies to you is local. Most cities require a business license or business tax certificate - usually $25-$150 a year, filed online in twenty minutes. If you run the company from your house, some cities also want a cheap home occupation permit on top of it.

The mistake that gets new haulers fined is licensing only in their home city. Plenty of cities require a license from any business performing work inside city limits, even one based elsewhere. Our rule: the first time a new city starts showing up in your job list, call its finance or licensing office and ask - "I called and asked" buys a surprising amount of goodwill with code enforcement. If you work unincorporated areas, check the county's requirements the same way.

Waste hauler rules: franchises, dump accounts, and the paper trail

Junk removal lives next door to a heavily regulated industry - scheduled solid waste collection - and you need to know which side of the line you are on. The weekly trash can is usually locked up by exclusive franchise agreements between cities and large haulers. One-time, customer-initiated bulky item removal - what you do - is exempt in most places, but not all: some cities, especially in California and the Pacific Northwest, write franchise rules broadly enough to sweep in construction debris or require a non-exclusive hauler permit, and some counties want a hauler registration that runs $50-$500 a year. One call to the solid waste department settles it for your market.

Then set up disposal like a business instead of a homeowner. Open a commercial account at your nearest transfer station or landfill; gate rates in 2026 typically run $40-$90 per ton. Keep every scale ticket. Those receipts are your proof of legal disposal (several states trace illegally dumped loads back to the hauler, with fines that start around $1,000 and climb into felony territory), your tax records, and your job-costing data. Disposal usually eats 10-20% of revenue, which is why it gets baked into your flat rates from day one.

The refusal list: what we never load

Hazardous materials are where new haulers get hurt, legally and physically. Landfills refuse them, your general liability policy excludes pollution claims, and transporting some of them without a license violates state and federal law. The customer will offer you an extra $50 to "just take it." The answer is no, every time.

Hard no - refer it to the county

  • Wet paint, stains, and solvents (fully dried-out latex cans are usually fine - confirm locally)
  • Motor oil, gasoline, antifreeze, and other automotive fluids
  • Propane tanks and any pressurized cylinder
  • Pesticides, herbicides, and pool chemicals
  • Asbestos or anything you suspect contains it - pre-1980s flooring, pipe wrap, siding
  • Medical waste and sharps
  • Ammunition, firearms, and fireworks
  • Unlabeled drums or containers of mystery liquid - do not even open them

The script we use on site: "We're not licensed to transport that, but your county household hazardous waste facility takes it for free." Every county has one and most homeowners have never heard of it, so you come off as the professional instead of the guy who said no. A refused $50 add-on costs nothing next to a contaminated-load fee at the scale ($100-$500) or an environmental violation with your name on it.

Needs a plan, not a no

  • Refrigerators, freezers, and AC units: federal law requires EPA-certified refrigerant recovery before disposal. Scrap yards and appliance recyclers handle it - charge the customer a $25-$50 line item.
  • Tires: most landfills charge $3-$15 per tire. Pass it through.
  • Mattresses: California, Connecticut, Rhode Island, and Oregon run recycling programs with free or cheap drop-off.
  • TVs and electronics: banned from landfills in roughly half the states; e-waste recyclers often take them free.
  • Car batteries: scrap yards will actually pay you for these.

Price these as line items on the quote instead of eating the fee. Customers accept "the landfill charges for tires, so we do too" without blinking.

The one-afternoon setup, in order

  1. File the LLC on your Secretary of State's website ($50-$150 in most states, approved within a week).
  2. Get the EIN at irs.gov the same day - it issues instantly.
  3. Open a business checking account with the LLC approval and EIN letter.
  4. Quote general liability with Next, Thimble, and Hiscox; buy the $1M/$2M policy and download your COI.
  5. Call your auto insurer and move the truck onto a commercial policy before the first paid load.
  6. Buy the city business license and ask about home occupation permits while you are on the phone.
  7. Call the county solid waste department: confirm whether you need a hauler permit, and open a scale account at the transfer station.

Total elapsed time is about a week, most of it waiting on the state. Once done, it stays done - the license renews once a year, the COI lives on your phone, and your hours go to the parts of the business that actually pay.
