How to Quote Junk Removal From a Photo (Operator's Photo-Quote System)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

A customer texts you a blurry photo of a garage packed to the ceiling and asks, "What would you charge to haul all this?" If you fumble the answer, you lose the job to whoever replies first with a confident number. If you guess too low, you show up, load for two hours, and realize you priced a half-truck job at quarter-truck money. Learning how to quote junk removal from a photo is the single fastest way to win more jobs without driving all over town for free estimates.

After thousands of jobs, our crew quotes roughly 70-80% of work straight from photos and a few text questions. We close the rest on site. This is the exact system we use: how to read the photo, how to estimate load fraction, the text script that gets you the missing details, and how to confirm the price when you arrive so you never eat a bad guess.

Why photo quoting beats free on-site estimates

Driving to a free estimate costs you 45-90 minutes and $8-15 in fuel before you've earned a dollar. Do five of those a week and you've burned a full workday on jobs you may not even book. Photo quoting flips that math. You answer in minutes, hold your margin, and only roll the truck when there's a confirmed price and a confirmed customer.

It also filters tire-kickers. People who want a real quote will send a photo. People who just want to argue about price usually won't. That self-selection alone saves hours every week. If you're still figuring out the fundamentals, our guide to starting a junk removal business covers the setup that makes photo quoting possible in the first place.

Step 1: Price by load fraction, not by item

The industry standard is volume-based pricing: you charge by how much of your truck or trailer bed the junk fills. Almost everyone in this trade prices in fractions of a load - minimum, 1/8, 1/4, 1/3, 1/2, 5/8, 3/4, 7/8, and full. Your job when reading a photo is to translate the pile into one of those fractions.

First, know your own container's cubic capacity cold. A common dump trailer or truck bed runs 12-16 cubic yards. A full load is whatever fits level with the rails, compacted reasonably. Every fraction is a slice of that. If you haven't locked in your numbers yet, our junk removal pricing guide walks through building a price card for your exact rig, and best trailer for junk removal helps you pick capacity that's easy to estimate against.

Reading the photo for volume

Look for a reference object you know the size of: a standard interior door is about 80 inches tall, a kitchen trash bag is roughly 30 gallons, a standard pallet is 48x40 inches. Use those to mentally scale the pile. Then estimate the footprint (length x width of the floor it covers) and the height, and convert to cubic yards (1 cubic yard = 27 cubic feet).

A pile 6 feet long, 4 feet wide, and 3 feet tall is 72 cubic feet, about 2.7 cubic yards - roughly 1/5 of a 14-yard load, so you'd quote it at 1/4. Round up, never down. Photos compress depth and almost always hide the stuff behind the front row.

Step 2: The text script that fills the gaps

One photo is never enough. The camera lies about depth, and customers crop out the worst of it. You need a tight script that gets the missing facts without 20 back-and-forth messages. Here's the one we use, copy-paste ready:

  • "Thanks for the photo! A few quick questions so I can give you an accurate price."
  • "Is what's in the photo everything, or is there more out of frame / in other rooms?"
  • "Any of these: appliances with freon (fridge, freezer, AC), pianos, hot tubs, paint, tires, or chemicals?"
  • "What floor is it on, and is it a straight carry-out or stairs/tight access?"
  • "What's your zip and when were you hoping to have it gone?"

Those five lines catch the things that wreck a quote: hidden volume, heavy or restricted items, and labor-killing access. A 1/2 load on the ground floor and a 1/2 load up two flights of stairs are not the same job, and your price should say so.

Send the price as a range, then anchor it

Reply with a tight range and the condition that locks it: "Based on the photo and your answers, that looks like about a 1/2 load - $300 to $360. I confirm the exact price on site before we start, and you approve it before we touch anything. Want me to get you scheduled?" The range protects you, the on-site confirmation protects them, and the close asks for the booking. For more on turning inquiries into booked jobs, see how to get junk removal customers.

Step 3: How to avoid underquoting

Underquoting is the number one profit killer for new operators. You feel pressure to be the cheap option, you read the photo optimistically, and you eat the difference in time and dump fees. Here's how to stop:

  1. Always round the fraction up. If it's between 1/4 and 1/2, quote 1/2. The behind-the-pile junk and the closet you can't see will make up the difference.
  2. Weight matters as much as volume. A 1/4 load of pillows and a 1/4 load of bagged concrete are wildly different. Tile, brick, dirt, shingles, and wet wood eat your dump-fee margin - add a heavy-material surcharge.
  3. Price the labor, not just the cube. Disassembly, long carries, stairs, and hoarding-level packing all add time. A photo showing a clear path is worth more than one showing a maze.
  4. Flag freon appliances every time. Refrigerators, freezers, and AC units contain refrigerant that must be recovered by an EPA-certified technician before disposal - you never cut the lines yourself. Either subcontract recovery or charge an add-on that covers the certified handling and the transfer-station fee.
  5. Build dump fees into the card. Transfer-station and landfill tipping fees keep climbing. Know your cost per yard and bake it in so a heavy load never turns into a break-even job.

Step 4: Confirm the price on site

The photo quote is a range, not a final invoice. Every quote you send must include the on-site confirmation step, and you must actually do it - walking it back after you've started loading is how you get bad reviews. The process is simple:

  1. Walk the full job with the customer before you unload a single tool. Look in the rooms, the closets, the side yard - everywhere the photo didn't show.
  2. Point at the pile and state the fraction out loud: "This is right where I figured - call it a half load at $330." Get a verbal yes.
  3. If it's bigger than the photo showed, say so calmly and re-quote before starting: "There's more here than the photo had - it's closer to a 3/4 load, so $475. Still good?" Most people say yes because you're being straight with them.
  4. Get approval in writing - a quick text confirmation or a signed line on your invoice. Then load.

This habit does two things. It eliminates the awkward end-of-job price fight, and it trains customers to send better photos next time because they know the on-site number is real.

Turn the confirmed job into a review

Before you pull away, ask for the review while you're standing there: "If you were happy, a quick Google review really helps a small local crew like us." Have them leave it from their own phone - reviews must come from the customer's own device and account, never one you control. A steady drip of honest reviews is what makes the next photo quote close itself. More on that in junk removal marketing ideas.

Where to advertise so the photos come to you

Photo quoting only works if people have an easy way to text you a photo. Put a clickable phone number everywhere. A few channels that actually generate photo inquiries:

  • Google Business Profile. Free, and the highest-intent leads in this business. People searching "junk removal near me" are ready to send a photo today.
  • Craigslist. Service posts cost $5 each and go under a labor/moving category - they're no longer free, but $5 for a steady trickle of "what would you charge for this" texts is cheap lead flow.
  • Facebook. You can't list a service as an item on Marketplace - it's against the rules. Instead run a Facebook business Page and post helpful answers in local buy/sell and community groups where people ask for haulers.
  • Property managers and Realtors. These accounts send you photos of cleanouts on repeat. Building property manager cleanout accounts turns one relationship into a dozen photo quotes a month.

Protect yourself before you quote anything

A clean quoting system means nothing if a job goes sideways and you're not covered. General liability insurance for a small hauling operation typically runs about $40-90 a month, and you'll need commercial auto coverage on the truck - personal auto policies usually exclude business hauling. And to be clear: forming an LLC is a business structure, not a license. It limits liability but it doesn't give you permission to operate. Check what your city and state actually require. Put it on repeat

Photo quoting is a skill, and it compounds. Your first month, you'll second-guess every fraction. By month three, you'll glance at a garage photo, fire back five questions and a range, and book the job before your competitor has finished typing. Keep a simple log: photo, your quote, the actual load on site. Compare them weekly. That feedback loop tightens your eye faster than anything else, and a tight eye is what keeps an example income range from turning into a loss.

Whether you're running this as a junk removal side hustle on weekends or building toward full-time, the operators who win are the ones who quote fast, quote accurately, and confirm on site every single time.
