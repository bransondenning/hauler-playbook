Junk Removal Contracts and Paperwork: The Forms That Win Commercial Accounts

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Most haulers think paperwork is the boring part of this business. It is the opposite. The guy with a clean quote, a signed waiver, a real invoice, and a folder of before-and-after photos is the guy who wins the apartment complex, keeps the property manager, and gets paid on net-30 without chasing anybody. The hauler who works on a handshake and a Venmo request stays stuck doing single-driveway jobs forever.

I run an operating junk removal company. Below is the exact stack of documents we use, why each one exists, and how it turns a one-time $200 garage job into a commercial account that bills the same crew every month. None of this requires a lawyer on retainer or expensive software. You can build the whole system on free templates and a phone camera.

Why paperwork is the moat around commercial accounts

Residential customers buy on price and speed. A property manager, a general contractor, or a real estate office buys on trust and accountability. They are not spending their own money, so they need a paper trail that protects them when their boss asks "who did this and what did we pay?" If you can hand them clean documents, you have removed the single biggest reason they would say no.

That is the whole game. The chains that charge $600 to $850 a load do not win commercial work because they are better haulers. They win it because they show up looking like a vendor a business can put on the books. You can look exactly that professional for the cost of a few PDF templates. The five documents every hauler needs

You do not need fifty forms. You need five, done well. Here is the full stack, in the order a job actually moves.

1. The quote (your first impression in writing)

A verbal "yeah, probably two hundred bucks" is how you lose money and trust at the same time. A written quote does three jobs: it sets the price in writing, it lists exactly what is and is not included, and it makes you look like a business instead of a guy with a truck.

Your quote should include: your business name and contact info, the date and a quote-expiration window (we use 14 days), the service address, an itemized scope (for example "1/2 trailer load, garage clearout, includes labor, hauling, dump fees, and sweep-out"), the flat price, and a short list of add-ons that cost extra. That last part matters. If a fridge is +$45 and a mattress is +$25, say so up front. Surprises on the invoice are how you get a one-star review.

For pricing the load itself, work off a real flat-rate card so every quote is consistent. Our junk removal pricing guide breaks down load-size pricing, add-ons, and the upcharges (stairs, long carry, same-day) that should live on every quote.

2. The service agreement (the contract that holds the relationship)

For a one-off residential job, a signed quote is usually enough. For anything recurring or commercial, you want a real service agreement. This is the document that turns a customer into an account. It does not need to be ten pages of legalese. A solid one-page agreement covers:

  • Scope of work — what you haul, what you don't (no hazardous waste, no wet paint, no chemicals without a separate quote).
  • Pricing structure — flat rate by load, or a locked per-unit-turnover rate for property managers.
  • Payment terms — due on completion, or net-15/net-30 for established commercial accounts.
  • Scheduling and access — who provides gate codes, where the dumpster or pile lives, lead time needed.
  • Cancellation and rescheduling — your notice window and any trip-charge for a locked-out crew.
  • Liability and limitation — you are not responsible for pre-existing property damage or items removed by mistake when the customer marked them for removal.

One clause that earns its keep: a "removal authorization" line stating the signer confirms they have the authority to dispose of everything on site. This protects you when a tenant later claims you tossed their stuff. The property manager signed off; the liability is theirs.

3. The liability waiver (the document that keeps you out of court)

Heavy lifting, stairs, narrow doorways, and old appliances mean things occasionally get scratched, dented, or dropped. A liability waiver does not make you immune, but it sets clear expectations and gives you a documented position. Your waiver should state that the customer accepts normal wear-and-tear risk of moving large items through their space, that you are not liable for damage to items the customer asked you to move within the property (versus haul away), and that pre-existing damage is documented with photos before work starts.

The waiver and the photos work together. When you document a scuffed wall before you touch anything, a later "you damaged my wall" claim evaporates. This is exactly why your phone is as important as your dolly.

4. The photo documentation file (your cheapest insurance)

This is the single highest-ROI habit in the business, and it costs nothing. On every job, you take:

  • Before photos of the full space and any existing damage, timestamped.
  • During photos of anything unusual (hidden hazards, a pre-cracked tile, a wall ding that was already there).
  • After photos of the cleared, swept space.

Before-and-after photos do three things at once. They prove the work to a property manager who never saw the unit. They kill damage disputes. And they become your best marketing — a clean turnover photo set sells the next account better than any ad. We attach the after-photos right to the invoice. 5. The invoice (how you actually get paid)

A professional invoice is the difference between "Venmo me whenever" and getting paid on a schedule a business respects. Your invoice needs: an invoice number, the date, your business and customer info, the itemized work performed (matching the quote), any add-ons, the total, the payment terms, and accepted payment methods. For commercial accounts, the invoice number and net terms are non-negotiable — their accounting department literally cannot pay you without them.

Paperwork is not a license — and other things people confuse

Two things haulers constantly mix up. First, forming an LLC is not a license to operate, and a contract is not insurance. An LLC is a legal structure that separates your personal assets from the business. It does not give you permission to haul, and it does not pay for a damaged floor. For that you carry general liability insurance, which typically runs around $40 to $90 a month for a small hauler, plus commercial auto on your truck or tow vehicle (a personal auto policy will not cover a business-use accident). Commercial clients will often ask for a certificate of insurance before they let you on the property, so this is not optional once you go after accounts.

Second, some loads have legal handling requirements your paperwork should reference. Refrigerant appliances — fridges, freezers, window AC units, dehumidifiers — contain refrigerant that, by EPA rules, must be recovered by an EPA-certified technician. You never cut the lines yourself. Your service agreement should note that refrigerant appliances are handled through certified recovery and may carry an added fee. It is a small line that signals you actually know the rules, which is exactly what a property manager wants to see.

How to set up the system without overpaying

You can run this entire stack for nearly nothing:

  1. Build five templates once. A quote, a one-page agreement, a waiver, an invoice, and a photo-folder naming system. A free invoicing app or a simple PDF set covers it. Reuse them on every job.
  2. Get a Google Business Profile. Free, and the biggest local-search lever you have. When you finish a job and the customer is thrilled, the review should come from the customer's own device — never from your phone or a shared link you control, which violates the rules and can get reviews wiped.
  3. List your services correctly. A Craigslist services post costs $5 and goes under the labor/move category — it is not free, but it is cheap and it converts. On Facebook, do not try to list a service as a Marketplace item (that is against the rules and gets you removed); instead run a Facebook business Page and post in local buy/sell and community groups.
  4. Track every job in a simple sheet. Date, customer, documents on file, paid yes/no. This is your accounts-receivable system and it costs zero dollars.

What clean paperwork is actually worth

Here is the math that should convince you. A single residential job might net you $150 to $400. A commercial account — an apartment complex doing unit turnovers, a contractor needing debris hauled, a real estate office clearing foreclosures — can mean several jobs a month, every month, on a locked rate. As an example, landing two recurring property-manager accounts at a few turnovers each can add a steady four-figure stream to a typical month's revenue. Income like this is an example of what's possible, not a guarantee — your market, pricing, and follow-through decide the real number.

The thing standing between you and that recurring revenue is almost never your ability to haul. It is whether you look like a vendor a business can trust. Five clean documents and a folder of timestamped photos do that for you. They are the cheapest competitive advantage in this entire trade, and most of your competitors will never bother to build them.
