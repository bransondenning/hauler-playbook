Junk Removal Side Hustle: $800-$900 a Week Around a 9-5

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company. Before it paid all the bills, it was a weekend operation: a pickup truck, a used trailer, and a phone that buzzed with photo quotes during the day job. That version - the junk removal side hustle - is still the best way we know to add $2,500-$3,500 a month in gross revenue without quitting anything.

This is the weekend operator playbook: the exact math behind an $800-$900 week, what the schedule actually looks like around a 9-5, the stuff that blows up a Saturday if you don't plan for it, and the specific triggers that tell you it's time to go full-time. All figures are realistic 2026 US numbers based on how we operate. Treat them as examples, not promises - your dump fees, fuel costs, and local demand will move every number on this page.

Why junk removal beats most side hustles

Most side hustles pay you by the hour and cap out fast. Driving for a delivery app nets most people $15-$22 an hour after gas and wear on their car. Junk removal flips that math because you sell outcomes, not hours:

  • High ticket sizes. A minimum pickup runs $150. A full trailer load runs $400-$450 in most mid-cost US markets. One Saturday job can out-earn a full delivery shift.
  • You get paid same-day. Card, cash, Zelle, or Venmo on the driveway. No invoicing 30 days out, no platform holding your money.
  • Demand peaks on weekends anyway. Customers want junk gone when they're home - Saturday morning is prime time. A weekend-only operator isn't working the leftovers; they're working the rush.
  • Startup cost is low if you own a truck. Roughly $2,000-$4,000 all-in gets you operational. Compare that to almost any franchise or e-commerce play.
  • The skill bar is honest. Lift heavy things, text people back fast, show up when you said you would. That's 90% of it. The other 10% is pricing, which we'll cover next.

The weekend math: $850 gross from three jobs

The single biggest unlock for a part-time operator is flat-rate pricing by trailer fraction. You don't bill hours and you don't drive out to give estimates. The customer texts you photos of the pile, you compare it to your trailer, and you reply with a flat price - usually within the hour. Here's an example price card for a 6x12 trailer, in line with what we see across mid-cost US markets in 2026:

Minimum pickup (single item / small pile)$150
Quarter trailer$175
Half trailer$275
Three-quarter trailer$350
Full trailer$425

Your numbers should flex with your market - a high-cost metro can run 20-30% above this card, a rural area 10-15% below. We walk through building and stress-testing your own card in our junk removal pricing guide, but the card above is enough to run the weekend math.

The target for a weekend operator is simple: two trailer loads hauled plus one minimum-charge pickup. A realistic Saturday looks like this:

  • 8:00 am - garage cleanout, full trailer load: $425. Two hours of loading, then straight to the transfer station.
  • 12:30 pm - basement cleanout, half load: $275. On the way there, you swing by a minimum-charge appliance pickup: $150 that you routed onto the same run. Half load plus the appliance go to the dump together - one trip, two tickets.
  • Gross for the day: $850. Two dump runs, three jobs, done by 4:00 pm.

Now the part most side-hustle articles skip - what it costs to earn that $850:

  • Disposal fees: $120-$160 for two mixed household loads at typical 2026 transfer station rates ($60-$95 per ton, with most full trailer loads weighing 1,200-2,500 lbs).
  • Fuel: $30-$40 for a local route towing a loaded trailer.
  • Take-home before insurance and taxes: roughly $650-$700.

String together 45 working weekends a year at that pace and you're looking at $35,000-$38,000 in example gross side income, around $28,000 after direct job costs - before self-employment tax. Some weekends you'll book four jobs and beat it. Some rainy February Saturdays you'll book one. The average is what matters, and $800-$900 gross per week is an achievable average for a weekend operator in a town of 50,000+ once the phone is ringing.

What it costs to start (while keeping your day job)

Assuming you already own a half-ton pickup or SUV with a tow package, here's a realistic 2026 startup budget:

  • Used 6x12 utility trailer: $1,500-$3,000. You do not need an $9,000 dump trailer to start - that's a year-two upgrade you buy with revenue, not savings.
  • Equipment: ratchet straps, tarps, appliance dolly, work gloves, basic demo tools - $250-$400.
  • General liability insurance: $55-$100/month for a solo operator with $1M coverage. Do not skip this; one scratched hardwood floor without it erases a month of weekends.
  • LLC filing: $35-$500 depending on your state (most states fall in the $50-$150 range).
  • Marketing: nearly $0. Google Business Profile, a Facebook business page and local groups, and Nextdoor are free ($5 per Craigslist services post), and they're where junk removal customers actually look.

Call it $2,000-$4,000 all-in. At the example weekend rates above, that's a payback period of three to five working weekends - one of the fastest break-evens of any legitimate side business we know of.

Fitting it around a 9-to-5: the photo-quote system

The reason most service side hustles fail around a day job isn't the labor - it's the quoting. If every lead requires you to drive out, look at the pile, and shake a hand, you'll lose every job to whoever answered faster. The fix is the photo-quote system:

  1. Customer texts or messages you photos of the junk.
  2. You eyeball it against your trailer fractions and reply with a flat price - within the hour, every time.
  3. They confirm, you book a Saturday time window, done. No site visit, no haggling on the driveway.

That one system is what makes this compatible with a full-time job, because quoting happens from your phone on a lunch break. Here's the actual weekly time budget for a weekend operator:

  • Monday-Friday: 20-30 minutes a day total - answering leads at lunch, sending photo quotes after dinner, confirming bookings. Speed matters more than polish; the first business to reply wins roughly half of all marketplace leads.
  • Friday evening: 30 minutes to route Saturday's jobs, text every customer a confirmation, and check the transfer station's weekend hours.
  • Saturday: 7:00 am to mid-afternoon. Two or three jobs and your dump runs.
  • Sunday: optional. Some operators run a half day; we'd suggest keeping it as overflow and rest, because burnout is the real competitor here.

Total: 10-14 hours a week. In summer, you can add single-item evening pickups after work - a $150 minimum charge for 45 minutes of effort at 6:30 pm is a very good trade.

Filling the calendar without spending on ads

A weekend operator needs three booked jobs a week, not thirty. That's why you don't need paid ads to start. The free stack, in priority order:

  • Google Business Profile. Free, and it's where "junk removal near me" searches land. Set it up properly, get your first five reviews, and it becomes your best lead source within 60-90 days.
  • Facebook page and local groups. The highest-volume free channel for junk removal in 2026. Post twice a week with real before/after photos from your business page, shared into groups (Marketplace item listings are for physical products only).
  • Craigslist and Nextdoor. Lower volume, near-zero competition for fast responders.
  • The review flywheel. Ask every single customer for a Google review while you're still in the driveway. Ten reviews puts you ahead of most part-time competitors in a mid-size market.

We've written up the full channel-by-channel system, including what to say in the posts, in how to get junk removal customers. The short version: in month one, your job is to be the fastest responder in your zip code, not the biggest brand.

The things that blow up a weekend schedule

Operator reality that the YouTube highlight reels leave out:

  • Transfer station hours. Many close at 3:00 or 4:00 pm on Saturday and are closed Sunday. If your last load misses the gate, it sits on your trailer until next week - and so does your trailer. Route your day around the dump's clock, not the customers'.
  • Surcharge items. Mattresses ($15-$40 each at many facilities), tires, and anything with refrigerant ($25-$75 for freon recovery) carry extra disposal fees. Build them into your quote when you see them in the photos, not at the dump.
  • Weather. A rained-on sectional sofa weighs nearly double and your half-load quote doesn't change. Tarp loads, and quote curbside piles assuming they've been rained on once.
  • No-shows and ghosting. Confirm every job by text the morning of. A dead 10:00 am slot on a three-job Saturday takes a third of your day's revenue with it.
  • Overbooking. Three jobs is a full Saturday for a solo operator. The fourth job you squeeze in is the one where you get sloppy, scratch a door frame, and earn your first bad review.

Weekday revenue without touching your PTO: property managers

Here's the move that separates a side hustle from a business: property-manager accounts. A property manager with a vacant rental unit is losing $40-$80 a day in rent for every day it sits, and they cannot re-list it until the previous tenant's junk is gone. They don't shop around - they call whoever answered last time.

Two things make PMs perfect for a weekend operator. First, vacant-unit cleanouts can happen on weeknight evenings, because nobody lives there - that's revenue that doesn't touch your Saturday capacity. Second, they repeat: a single small property manager can generate one to three cleanouts a month at $275-$425 each, year-round, with zero marketing cost.

The pitch that works is simple: same-day or next-day photo quotes, cleanouts within 48 hours, and a 20%-off-for-life rate for management companies. You're trading margin for a recurring account, and it's the best trade on this page. When to go full-time (the actual triggers)

Don't quit on a feeling. Quit on numbers. Here are the four triggers we'd want to see, in order:

  1. You're turning away more work than you're booking. If you've been booked solid two to three weekends out for six straight weeks, demand isn't your problem anymore - capacity is.
  2. Side gross of $4,000-$5,000 a month for three consecutive months. Not one great month. Three boring, repeatable ones, including at least one slow-season month.
  3. Repeat and commercial work is 25%+ of revenue. Property managers, realtors, and contractors are the revenue that shows up in January when homeowner demand doesn't.
  4. Three to six months of personal expenses saved, plus a health insurance plan. Marketplace coverage for a solo operator commonly runs $400-$700 a month in 2026 - price it before you resign, not after.

Run the replacement math honestly. To match a $65,000 W-2 salary with benefits, a solo operator typically needs $110,000-$130,000 in gross revenue - self-employment tax takes 15.3%, health insurance is on you, there's no paid time off, and the trailer and truck need a repair reserve. That's roughly $9,000-$11,000 a month gross, which usually means five to six days a week of work or a first hire. Plenty of full-time solo operators get there; just know that's the bar.

And here's the part nobody says: it's completely fine to never go full-time. A junk removal side hustle that grosses $30,000+ a year on 12 hours a week, indefinitely, while your day job covers benefits and retirement, is a great outcome - not a failure to scale.

Your first 90 days, weekend-only

  1. Days 1-7: File the LLC, bind general liability insurance, set up your Google Business Profile, buy the trailer and equipment, and write your price card.
  2. Days 8-14: Post on Facebook (business page + local groups), Craigslist ($5), and Nextdoor. Take any reasonable job the first weekend, even if it's a single $150 pickup - you need photos and a review more than you need a perfect route.
  3. Days 15-45: Build toward three-job Saturdays. Ask every customer for a Google review in the driveway. Track every job: gross, dump fees, fuel, hours.
  4. Days 46-90: If you're closing more than 80% of your photo quotes, your prices are too low - raise the card $25 across the board. Start property-manager outreach at ten contacts a week, and aim to land your first account before day 90.

That's the whole playbook: a price card, a photo-quote system, free lead channels, two loads and a minimum every Saturday, and PM accounts filling the weeknights. We built our company on exactly this sequence, and the numbers above are the same ones we'd use if we were starting again next weekend.
