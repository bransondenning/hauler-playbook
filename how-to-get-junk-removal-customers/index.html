How to Get Junk Removal Customers: 5 Free Channels Ranked by Speed

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Every week somebody asks us the same question: "I've got the truck, I've got the insurance, where are the customers?" Fair question. A junk removal business with no leads is just an expensive trailer parked in your driveway.

Here's the good news: junk removal is one of the few service businesses where you can realistically book your first paying job within 72 hours of deciding to start, without spending a dollar on advertising. Our first 50 jobs came almost entirely from free channels, and years later those same channels still produce the majority of our residential work. We didn't run paid ads at all in year one.

This guide covers the five free channels that actually work in 2026, ranked by speed - meaning how fast each one typically puts a paying customer on your schedule. If you haven't set up the business itself yet (entity, insurance, dump accounts, pricing), start with our guide on how to start a junk removal business and come back. 1. Craigslist: Your First Customer This Week

Yes, Craigslist still works in 2026 - specifically for junk removal. The people searching Craigslist services are exactly your customer: they have a problem ("this couch needs to be gone by Saturday"), they want a price fast, and they don't care about your branding. In most metros of 200,000+ people, a well-written ad can pull 3 to 8 inquiries a week. That's not a flood, but when your average ticket is $250-$350, two booked jobs a week from a channel that costs $5 a post is real money.

The 48-hour repost rhythm

Here's what most new operators miss: a Craigslist ad has a shelf life of about two days. After that it slides off the first page of results and your phone goes quiet. Craigslist lets you repost once 48 hours have passed (each US services post costs $5 - still the cheapest high-intent lead source going), so build this into your routine:

  • Write 2-3 different ad variants (different headlines, different lead photos, same phone number).
  • Every 48 hours, delete the oldest ad and repost a variant. Set a recurring phone reminder - Monday, Wednesday, Friday works.
  • Post in the Services section under labor/move (that's where hauling ads live). Don't blast the same ad into multiple categories; that's how ads get flagged and ghosted.

This rhythm keeps you on page one permanently. It takes ten minutes per repost. Most of your competitors post once, forget about it, and conclude "Craigslist is dead." It isn't - their ad is just buried on page four.

What goes in the ad

The ads that book jobs share three things: a price in the headline, a photo, and a fast way to get a quote. Something like "Junk Removal - $150 Minimum - Same Day - Text a Photo for a Flat Price" outperforms vague headlines every time. In the body, list your flat-rate card so people can self-quote. Ours looks like this: $150 minimum, $175 quarter trailer, $275 half, $350 three-quarter, $425 full. Then close with the photo-quote offer: "Text a photo of your pile and get a flat price within the hour." That one line does more work than everything else combined, because it removes the friction of scheduling an in-person estimate. If you haven't built your price card yet, our junk removal pricing guide walks through how to set rates that cover your dump fees and still win the job.

One more thing: answer fast. Craigslist customers message three or four haulers at once, and the first credible reply with a price usually wins. We aim for under 15 minutes during business hours. If you reply in four hours, you're quoting a job somebody else already booked.

2. Facebook Page and Local Groups: The Volume Play

For most new operators, Facebook becomes the number one free lead source in months one through three. It works two ways, and you need both.

Your business page (and why not Marketplace)

One caution first: Facebook Marketplace item listings are reserved for physical products - service listings get removed under the commerce rules, so don't burn your account on one. The durable play is a free business page: load it with your best photos - the trailer, a before/after, you looking like a human being and not a stock photo - paste your full flat-rate card and the photo-quote line into the About section, and pin a launch post to the top. The page is what people check before they message you, it ranks in Facebook search, and it costs nothing and runs in the background indefinitely.

Local groups: where the real money is

Join every active group in your service area: buy/sell/trade groups, "[Town] Community" groups, neighborhood pages, local mom groups if they allow service posts. Ten to fifteen groups is a reasonable target for a typical suburb-plus-city service area.

Then understand the actual play, because it's not what most people do. The play is not spamming your ad into every group daily - that gets you banned and, worse, makes the neighborhood associate your name with annoyance. The play is being the first helpful answer when someone posts "ISO junk removal" or "Can anyone recommend someone to haul off a hot tub?" Those posts go up constantly, and whoever answers first with a price, a photo, and a real profile usually gets the call.

  • Turn on notifications for your highest-traffic groups, or check them at set times (morning, lunch, evening).
  • Reply with substance: "We can do that - hot tubs typically run $275-$350 depending on access. Text a photo to [number] and I'll give you an exact flat price today."
  • When someone tags a competitor in a recommendation thread, don't pile on with "we do that too!" It reads as desperate. Be first instead.

You can also post your own content - an introduction post when you join (if rules allow), then a before/after post roughly monthly per group. More on what those posts should look like in the photo section below.

3. Nextdoor: Slow Burn, Highest Trust

Nextdoor leads take longer to show up, but they're the best leads on this list. A Nextdoor recommendation comes neighbor-to-neighbor, so by the time someone contacts you, they're half-sold. Price resistance is noticeably lower than Craigslist - these customers are buying trust, not hunting for the $80 guy.

The setup is simple: claim your free Nextdoor business page, fill out every field, add photos, and post a short introduction to your own neighborhood feed. What happens next is the part you can't force but can absolutely engineer: the recommendation flywheel.

When someone posts "Anyone know a good junk hauler?", Nextdoor surfaces businesses that neighbors have recommended. One genuine recommendation in a neighborhood tends to produce a string of jobs in that same neighborhood, because people see a name their actual neighbor vouched for. So the system is:

  1. Finish every job clean - swept driveway, photo sent to the customer.
  2. Ask happy customers specifically: "If you're on Nextdoor, a recommendation there helps us more than anything." Send the ask by text the same day, while the goodwill is fresh.
  3. Prioritize this ask in neighborhoods you want more work in. Five recommendations spread across five neighborhoods beats fifteen in one.

Skip Nextdoor's paid neighborhood ads in your first year. Organic recommendations carry the channel, and your ad budget - if you ever have one - works harder elsewhere.

4. Google Business Profile: The Engine That Compounds

This is the slowest channel on the list and, eventually, the most important. After 6-12 months of consistent effort, a strong Google Business Profile (GBP) typically becomes a junk removal company's single biggest lead source - in our experience and across most operators we know, it eventually outproduces everything else on this page combined. Someone types "junk removal near me," sees the map pack, calls one of the top three results. That's the whole game, and reviews decide who's in the top three.

Set it up right in one sitting

  • Create the profile as a service-area business and hide your home address. List the towns you actually serve.
  • Primary category: "Junk removal service." Add secondary categories that fit (debris removal, hauling).
  • Fill out the services section with real prices - "Quarter trailer load: $175" - because prices in the profile pre-qualify callers.
  • Upload 10+ real photos on day one: trailer, crew, completed jobs. Add new photos weekly; profiles with fresh photos get measurably more calls.

The reviews engine

Verification can take a couple of weeks, and ranking takes longer - which is exactly why you start day one, not month three. Then run the review system with zero exceptions:

  1. Same day as every completed job, text the customer: "Thanks again! If you were happy with the work, a Google review is the biggest favor you could do for a small business. Takes 60 seconds: [direct review link]."
  2. Target your first 10 reviews inside 30 days. Ten reviews is roughly the threshold where you stop looking like a fly-by-night operation and start appearing in map results.
  3. After that, steady beats spiky. Google rewards consistent review velocity - a realistic 2-4 new reviews a month from normal job flow looks far healthier than 20 in one week and then silence.
  4. Reply to every review, and mention the service and town naturally: "Glad we could get that garage cleanout in Riverton done same-day." Those replies are indexable text working for you around the clock.

Realistic expectations: in a mid-size market, a profile with 25+ reviews, complete services, and weekly photos can plausibly generate 10-30 inbound calls a month by month six. Your market and competition will move that number, but the direction is reliable: this channel only grows if you feed it.

5. Before/After Photos: The Content That Feeds Everything

This isn't a separate lead channel so much as a multiplier on the other four. A garage stuffed to the ceiling next to a swept, empty garage sells junk removal better than any sentence ever written. It proves you're real, shows your standard of work, and - critically - teaches customers what a "half trailer" actually looks like, which makes your flat-rate pricing feel concrete instead of arbitrary.

Build a 30-second routine into every job:

  • Before: stand in one spot, shoot landscape, get the whole mess in frame. Do this before you touch anything.
  • After: same spot, same angle, after you've swept. The sweep matters - "broom clean" in the after photo is half the marketing.
  • Bonus shot: the loaded trailer. It visually justifies the price and doubles as proof of volume.

Then route the photos everywhere: into your rotating Craigslist ads, your pinned page post, monthly group posts, and weekly GBP uploads. A simple caption formula works: what it was, how long it took, what it cost. "Full garage cleanout in [town] - 2 hours, $350 flat rate, donated what we could." That last clause about donating earns more comments and shares than anything else we post - people care where their stuff goes.

One operator-to-operator note: always ask the customer before posting photos of their property, and never include house numbers or anything identifying. A one-line ask at the end of the job ("Mind if we use the before/after on our page? No address shown") gets a yes nearly every time.

Putting It Together: A Realistic First 30 Days

Run all five channels in parallel - they're sequenced by when they pay off, not by when you start them.

  1. Days 1-2: Write your flat-rate card. Post your first Craigslist ad ($5) and publish your Facebook page launch post. Create your Google Business Profile and start verification. Claim your Nextdoor business page.
  2. Days 3-7: Join 10-15 local Facebook groups. Start the 48-hour Craigslist repost rhythm. Answer every ISO post you see. First jobs typically land this week.
  3. Days 8-21: Run the review text after every job. Shoot before/afters on every job, no exceptions. Post your first before/after to groups and GBP.
  4. Days 22-30: Take stock: which channel produced which jobs? Double down on whatever's working in your market, keep everything else on maintenance.

A typical first month running this playbook in a decent market might produce 8-15 jobs - at a $250-$300 average ticket, that's roughly $2,000-$4,500 in revenue before dump fees and fuel. Some markets do better, some slower; the inputs are what you control.
