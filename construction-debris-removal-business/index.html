Construction Debris Removal Business: How to Win Contractor Accounts

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Construction debris is the bread-and-butter load most new haulers ignore because it's heavy, dusty, and the dump charges by the ton. That's exactly why it's a good business. Homeowners shop three quotes and ghost you. A remodeling contractor who trusts you to show up Thursday and haul the old kitchen so the floor crew can start Friday will call you fifty times a year and never ask what your competitor charges. This is the playbook for building a construction debris removal business around contractor accounts, priced by weight, run like a real operation.

Why contractor work beats one-off junk jobs

A single homeowner garage cleanout is a one-time transaction. A general contractor doing four remodels a quarter, a roofer tearing off three jobs a week in season, a flooring installer ripping out carpet on every install - those are recurring revenue. You're not marketing to them every month; you earn the relationship once and keep it with reliability.

The other advantage is predictability. Construction debris is mostly known material: drywall, lumber, flooring, roofing, fixtures, packaging. You can quote it fast and load it fast. Compare that to a hoarder house where you don't know what's behind the next door. If you're weighing this against general residential work, the broader tradeoffs are covered in how to start a junk removal business.

Weight-based pricing is the only thing that works here

Volume pricing (charging by how much of the trailer or truck bed you fill) is how most junk removal is sold, and it's fine for light, bulky household junk. Construction debris breaks that model. A trailer of drywall scrap can weigh 4,000 lbs and barely look half full. A trailer of broken concrete or tile will max your axle rating before it reaches the top rail. If you price that by volume, the dump scale eats your entire margin.

For contractor debris you price two ways and quote whichever protects you:

  • Per-ton pass-through plus a haul fee. You charge the contractor your actual disposal cost per ton plus a fixed haul/labor fee. Example: dump charges you $65/ton, you bill $95/ton plus a $150 minimum haul. Transparent, and contractors who buy materials by the ton already think this way.
  • Flat rate with a weight cap. You quote $X for the load up to a stated weight (say 2,000 lbs), with a clear per-100-lb overage. This works for repeat jobs where you know the typical tonnage.

Either way, the dump scale ticket is your source of truth. Keep every weigh ticket - it's how you prove your costs, set future quotes, and back up an invoice if a contractor questions it. Always set a job minimum

Even a single ripped-out vanity is worth a trip charge. A realistic 2026 job minimum is $125-$175 depending on your market. Below that, the drive, the dump run, and the fuel eat you alive. Contractors respect a minimum; they have one too.

Know your materials and your dump rules

The fastest way to lose money on construction debris is showing up at the transfer station with a mixed, contaminated load and getting turned away or surcharged. Different materials have different rules and different gates.

MaterialDisposal notePricing flag
Clean wood / lumberOften cheaper at a wood-recycler than the landfillLow per-ton, bulky
DrywallSome sites charge extra; gypsum is restricted in placesHeavy for the volume
Concrete, brick, tileTake to a clean-fill / aggregate recycler, not the landfillVery heavy - cap your loads
Roofing shinglesOften a separate rate; some sites need shingles-onlyExtremely heavy
Scrap metalScrap yard pays YOU - keep it separateNegative cost
Asbestos / lead / hazmatDo NOT haul. Licensed abatement onlyRefuse the job

Two hard lines. First, never knowingly haul asbestos-containing material, lead paint debris, or anything labeled hazardous - that requires licensed abatement contractors and you can face serious liability. If a contractor is vague about what's in the load, ask directly and put it in writing. Second, separate your scrap metal. Copper pipe, old appliances, aluminum, steel studs - a scrap yard pays you for that. Sorting metal out of a remodel load can turn a break-even job into a profitable one.

Refrigerant appliances have a federal rule

Remodels produce old fridges, freezers, AC units, and water heaters with sealed systems. Units containing refrigerant require EPA-certified refrigerant recovery before disposal - the refrigerant must be professionally evacuated. Never cut a line or vent it to the air; that's illegal and dangerous. Either you get certified, you partner with a shop that recovers it, or you take those units to a facility that handles recovery. Build the recovery fee into your appliance pricing.

The right rig for heavy material

Construction debris is a weight game, so your equipment is about axle rating, not just cubic feet. A 14-foot dump trailer with a solid GVWR and the right tow vehicle will out-earn a pickup on this work, because you can take a full legal load of heavy material and dump it without hand-unloading. Know your trailer's payload rating and your tow vehicle's capacity, and never exceed them - an overweight rig is a ticket, a wreck, and a destroyed reputation in one. The full breakdown of options is in best trailer for junk removal and the truck-vs-trailer cost comparison in junk removal truck vs trailer.

One practical note: a dump trailer that can drop a load at the transfer station in two minutes versus forking debris out by hand for twenty minutes is the difference between three jobs a day and five. On heavy material, unload speed is profit.

Insurance and registration before you touch a job site

Job sites are higher-risk than a residential driveway, and contractors will ask for proof of insurance before they let you on. Get a general liability policy - typical small-hauler GL runs roughly $40-$90 a month depending on your state, revenue, and coverage limits. You'll also need commercial auto on the tow vehicle; personal auto won't cover a business hauling operation, and a denied claim will end you.

Clear up one common confusion: forming an LLC is not a business license. An LLC is a legal structure for liability and taxes. Depending on your city or county you may still need a local business license or permit to operate, and your dump may require an account or hauler registration. Sort all of it before your first contractor job - the details are in junk removal license and insurance.

Landing recurring contractor accounts

You don't win these with a glossy ad. You win them by being the hauler who answers the phone and shows up. Here's the order of operations that actually fills a schedule.

  1. Walk the job sites. Drive your area, find active remodels and new builds, and hand the GC or super a card in person. This is the single highest-conversion thing you can do.
  2. Get a Google Business Profile. Contractors search "construction debris hauler near me" too. A complete GBP with photos of your rig and real reviews ranks you locally. Ask happy customers to leave a review from their own phone - you cannot post reviews for them, and review-gating schemes get profiles suspended.
  3. Use a Facebook business Page plus local contractor groups. A Page is where you post job photos and build credibility; local builder/contractor groups are where the referrals happen. Note that Facebook Marketplace forbids listing services as items - don't try to post your hauling as a Marketplace listing, you'll get pulled. Use the Page and groups instead.
  4. Post on Craigslist under labor/move. A Craigslist services post costs about $5 (it is not free) and belongs in the labor/move category, not for-sale. Cheap, and contractors and handymen still check it.
  5. Build a referral loop with adjacent trades. Flooring installers, remodelers, and roofers all generate debris and all know each other. One solid relationship becomes five.

Make it stupid-easy to rebook you

The whole value of a contractor account is that they don't have to re-shop you. Net-15 or net-30 invoicing, a consistent point of contact (you), and a standing "text me the address and I'll handle it" arrangement is what turns a first job into fifty. Send a clean invoice the same day with the weigh ticket attached. Professionalism on paper is rare in this trade, and it's why contractors stick.

What the money can look like

These are example figures, not guarantees - your market, your dump rates, and your hustle all move them. Suppose you run three contractor jobs a day, four days a week. At an average of $325 per job net of disposal, that's roughly $3,900 a week in revenue before your own costs. Subtract fuel, insurance, dump tonnage, and maintenance, and a lean solo operator can keep a healthy share of that - but a bad month, a slow building season, or a blown tire can swing it hard.

The contractor segment smooths those swings because the work repeats. For a grounded look at margins, overhead, and what eats your profit, read is junk removal business profitable. Your first 30 days

  1. Lock insurance: GL plus commercial auto. Get your certificate of insurance ready to email.
  2. Set up your dump accounts and confirm which sites take which materials, and at what per-ton rate.
  3. Build a simple weight-based price card with a job minimum and a per-ton overage.
  4. Stand up a Google Business Profile and a Facebook Page; post your rig and your first loads.
  5. Hand cards to ten active job sites this month. Track who calls back.
  6. Invoice same-day with the weigh ticket attached. Make rebooking effortless.

Construction debris removal isn't glamorous, but it's durable. Contractors don't churn the way homeowners do, the material is predictable, and weight-based pricing protects your margin if you respect the dump scale. Get the insurance right, refuse the loads you shouldn't touch, separate your scrap, and show up when you say you will. That's the whole business.
