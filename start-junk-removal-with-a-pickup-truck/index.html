How to Start a Junk Removal Business With a Pickup Truck (2026)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

If you own a half-ton pickup and a few hundred bucks, you already have most of what you need to take your first paid junk haul. I started this way, and so do most of the operators I know. A pickup is the lowest-cost legitimate entry into this trade. It won't do everything a dump trailer or box truck will, but it will get you cash-flowing fast and teach you the business before you sink money into equipment you might not need yet.

This is the honest version: what a truck can and can't haul, how much fits in the bed, where the first jobs come from, how to price when you're truck-only, and the exact signs that tell you it's time to bolt on a trailer.

What a pickup can and can't do

A standard short-bed pickup gives you roughly 50 to 65 cubic feet of usable bed space below the rails. Load above the rails with sideboards and you're realistically looking at one full standard "truck load" being about the same volume the franchises sell as a 1/4 to 1/3 truck. That's plenty for the bread-and-butter jobs: single-item pickups, garage corners, a couple pieces of furniture, busted patio sets, exercise equipment nobody uses.

Where a pickup struggles is weight and full-house volume. Your payload is the number that matters, not the cube. A half-ton is typically rated around 1,500 to 2,000 lbs of payload, and that includes you, your helper, and your tools. Wet carpet, tile, dirt, roofing shingles, and concrete will max out your weight long before the bed looks full. Overloading sags your suspension, blows tires, and is a great way to get pulled over. Know your truck's payload (it's on the door-jamb sticker) and respect it.

Stuff to leave alone at first

  • Refrigerant appliances (fridges, freezers, AC units, dehumidifiers, water coolers): the refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA Section 608-certified technician before disposal. Never cut or open the lines yourself. Either get certified, partner with someone who is, or charge a fee and route these to a facility that handles recovery.
  • Hazardous materials: paint, solvents, oil, propane tanks, chemicals, asbestos. These need special disposal and you are not equipped for them on day one.
  • Anything that exceeds your payload: a full hot tub, a piano, a load of dirt. Walk away or refer it out until you have the right rig.

A couple of notes that trip up new operators. An LLC is not a license to operate; it's a business structure that separates your personal assets from the business. You may still need a local business license or registration depending on your city or county. And don't run jobs on your personal auto policy. If you're hauling for pay and have a wreck, a personal policy can deny the claim. You want general liability plus proper commercial auto coverage. Where the first jobs come from

You don't need a website to land your first ten jobs. You need to be findable and reachable in the places people already look for haulers.

  1. Craigslist services. Post under labor/moving help. A services post is $5 in most markets now (it's not free anymore), but $5 to land a $150 job is the best ROI in this business. Re-post every few days so you stay near the top.
  2. Facebook the right way. Facebook Marketplace does not allow service listings posing as items, and posting your hauling "for sale" will get it pulled. Instead, set up a free Facebook business Page and post your service into local buy/sell, neighborhood, and community groups where it's allowed. That's where the steady residential calls come from.
  3. Google Business Profile. Set up a free GBP for your service area. This is your long-term lead engine. After every job, ask the happy customer to leave a review from their own phone (you can't post reviews on your own listing, and fake ones get your profile suspended). Real reviews from real customers are what make the phone ring six months from now.
  4. Word of mouth and yard signs. Tell everyone you know. A magnetic sign on the truck door plus a simple flyer at the laundromat still works.

How to price when you're truck-only

Junk removal is priced by volume, not by the hour or by the item, and you anchor that volume to how much fits in your truck. The standard franchise model sells fractions of a "truck load," and you should mirror it so customers can compare you to what they already know.

Your price card should cover, at minimum:

  • Minimum / single item: a floor price so a one-mattress job is still worth the drive.
  • 1/4 truck, 1/2 truck, 3/4 truck, full truck: tiered by how much of your bed is filled.
  • Surcharges: stairs, long carries, heavy materials, and special-handling items.

Here's an example range many small operators charge in 2026; your market, dump fees, and competition will move these numbers, so treat them as a starting point, not a promise:

  • Single item / minimum: roughly $90-$150
  • 1/4 truck: roughly $150-$250
  • 1/2 truck: roughly $250-$375
  • Full pickup load: roughly $400-$550

Build in your dump fee and your time. If a load is going to cost you $60 at the transfer station, that comes off the top before you've earned a dollar. Always quote with the disposal cost baked in. What the math can look like

On a decent residential day with a pickup, two or three jobs is realistic once you're booked. A few $150-$300 jobs in a day adds up quickly, but be honest with yourself about costs: fuel, dump fees, your insurance, and your own time. After expenses, a solo truck operator can clear a healthy day rate, but income is not guaranteed; it depends entirely on how many jobs you book and how tight your pricing and disposal costs are. The pickup's hard limits, and when to add a trailer

The truck-only phase is about proving you can sell and deliver the service. The ceiling shows up fast in two forms: trips and turned-down jobs.

Watch for these signals:

  • You're making two or three dump runs for a single big cleanout because the bed can't hold it.
  • You're declining full-house, estate, or construction-debris jobs because they don't fit, and those are the biggest tickets.
  • Loading and unloading by hand over the tailgate is eating your day, when a dump trailer would tip the load out in two minutes.

When those start costing you real money, a trailer is the upgrade, not a bigger truck. A dump trailer roughly triples to quadruples your volume per trip, slashes dump runs, and unlocks the high-dollar cleanouts. It's the single best ROI move in this business after you've validated demand. Your first-week checklist

  1. Confirm your truck's payload on the door-jamb sticker and buy a hand truck, straps, and a tarp.
  2. Get general liability and commercial auto quotes; don't haul for pay on a personal policy.
  3. Register your business (LLC and/or local license as your area requires).
  4. Build a simple volume-based price card with the dump fee baked in.
  5. Post a $5 Craigslist services ad, set up a free Facebook Page, and claim your Google Business Profile.
  6. Book your first job, do great work, and ask the customer to review you from their phone.
