Is a Junk Removal Business Profitable? The Real Profit Math

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Short answer: yes, a junk removal business can be profitable, and the margins are better than most trades. But "can be" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. We run a junk removal company, and we've watched operators with the same truck and the same market run wildly different numbers - one clearing real take-home pay, the other burning through dump fees and wondering where it went. The difference isn't luck. It's math and a few specific habits.

Let's walk the actual numbers, job by job and month by month, with realistic 2026 US figures. No "six figures from your driveway" nonsense.

The unit economics: what one job actually clears

Junk removal is priced by volume - how much of your truck or trailer the load fills. The revenue per job is high relative to your direct cost, which is what makes the model work. Here's a typical full trailer job broken down the way we think about it on a whiteboard.

Line itemFull trailer (example)
Revenue (flat rate)$425
Dump / disposal fee$75 - $120
Fuel (round trip to site + transfer station)$15 - $30
Direct cost subtotal$90 - $150
Gross margin per job~$275 - $335

That's before your time and before fixed overhead like insurance, but it shows why the model is attractive: on a single full load you're keeping roughly 65-75% as gross margin. Smaller loads are even better on a percentage basis because the disposal weight is lower while your minimum price holds the floor. A quarter-trailer job at $175 might only cost you $25-40 to dump and drive, leaving $135+ in gross margin for under an hour of work.

This is exactly why we price flat-rate by trailer fraction instead of hourly or by-the-item. A simple price card - say $150 minimum / $175 quarter / $275 half / $350 three-quarter / $425 full - lets you quote in seconds and protects your margin on the easy loads. Monthly scenarios: part-time vs full-time

Per-job margin is the engine; volume is the gas pedal. Here's where most "is it profitable" questions actually get answered - it depends entirely on how many jobs you run and what they average.

Part-time / side hustle (evenings and weekends)

Say you run 3 jobs a week, averaging $250 in revenue (a mix of minimums and half-loads). That's roughly 12-13 jobs a month and around $3,100 in revenue. After disposal, fuel, and your monthly fixed costs (we'll detail those below), a part-timer in our experience typically nets somewhere in the $1,800-$2,400 range. Not life-changing, but it's real cash for a few hours on Saturdays, and it's a genuine test of whether you like the work before you quit anything. We cover the realistic ramp in our junk removal side hustle breakdown.

Full-time, one truck, owner-operator

Now run 4-6 jobs a day, 5 days a week. At an average ticket of $275 and, say, 22 jobs a week, that's around $6,000/week or roughly $24,000-$26,000 a month in revenue. Direct costs (dump + fuel) typically run 20-30% of revenue, so call it $6,000-$7,500. Subtract fixed overhead and any part-time helper, and a busy solo or two-person operation can typically clear $9,000-$13,000/month in owner profit. The wide range is the honest part - it swings hard on your job mix, how dense your routes are, and how disciplined your pricing is.

Note what is not in those numbers: a salary you're paying yourself separate from "profit," equipment replacement reserves, and slow winter weeks. Build those in before you decide the business is buying you a boat.

The costs that quietly eat your margin

The reason two identical trucks produce different bank balances comes down to the costs people underestimate. Here are the real ones.

  • Dump fees. Your single biggest variable cost. Transfer stations charge by weight or by yard, and a heavy load (think wet carpet, dirt, shingles, plaster) can blow past $120. Light, bulky loads cost less to dump than they look. Learn your local rates per ton before you set prices - this single number decides whether your full-trailer rate makes sense.
  • Fuel. Modest per job, but it compounds with bad routing. Two trips across town because you didn't batch nearby jobs is pure margin gone.
  • Insurance. General liability typically runs $40-$90/month for a small hauler, and you genuinely need it - one scratched hardwood floor or dinged garage door costs more than a year of premiums. Note that forming an LLC is not a license; many cities and counties require a separate hauler or waste-transporter permit, and an LLC does nothing to satisfy that. Check both. We go deeper in our junk removal license and insurance guide.
  • Labor. The moment you add a helper at $18-$25/hour, your per-job math changes. Helpers are how you scale revenue, but they also turn a fat margin into a normal one if your pricing doesn't account for them. Price the labor in, or only staff up on heavy jobs.
  • Wear, maintenance, and your time. Trailers, tires, straps, and the truck pulling it all wear out. Set aside a few percent of revenue so a blown trailer axle isn't a crisis.

None of these are deal-breakers. They're just the difference between the revenue number you brag about and the profit number that's actually yours.

Where the extra profit hides

Here's the part new operators miss: a chunk of your load isn't garbage - it's inventory. The same trailer of "junk" can throw off three extra revenue streams beyond the haul fee.

  • Scrap metal. Appliances, water heaters, steel, copper, and aluminum have real value at the scrapyard. One important rule: refrigerators, freezers, and AC units contain refrigerant, and EPA rules require certified recovery before you scrap them. Never cut a refrigerant line yourself - either pay a certified tech to recover it (they tag the unit) or take it to a yard that handles recovery. Get this wrong and the fines dwarf the scrap value.
  • Resale. Solid furniture, tools, exercise equipment, and decent electronics can be flipped on Facebook Marketplace or to a local secondhand store. A single $80 dresser resale can outearn the haul fee on that job.
  • Donations. Usable goods to a charity that issues receipts keep them out of the paid dump stream (saving disposal cost) and can support a tax deduction. Lower cost is profit.

We've had loads where scrap and resale added $60-$150 on top of the haul fee. It's not every job, but over a month it's a meaningful line on the P&L - and it's margin your competitors who "just dump everything" leave on the table.

What actually separates profitable operators from broke ones

Same market, same equipment, different outcomes. After years of this, the dividing lines are consistent.

1. Flat-rate pricing, quoted fast

Operators who price hourly or "figure it out on site" lose money to slow loads and awkward negotiations. The profitable ones use a fixed price card by trailer fraction and a photo-quote system: the customer texts a photo of their pile, and you reply with a flat price within the hour. It's faster for them, it locks your margin, and the speed alone wins jobs - most people book whoever answers first with a real number. If you're choosing equipment to price around, our best trailer for junk removal rundown pairs well with this approach.

2. Recurring revenue from property managers

One-off residential jobs are good money but unpredictable. The operators with steady, profitable books land property-manager cleanout accounts. Property managers feel real pain from vacancy days - every day a unit sits full of a former tenant's junk is lost rent - so a hauler who shows up same-day and bills cleanly is worth a lot to them. A simple offer like 20% off for life in exchange for becoming their go-to gets you repeat work that fills your weekday schedule without spending a dime on ads. A handful of PM accounts can be the difference between hunting for jobs and just running a route.

3. Getting found cheaply

Profit dies fast if every job costs you $40 in ads. The cheap, durable channels: a free Google Business Profile that actually ranks (ask each customer to leave a review from their own phone using a link you text them - and ignore anyone promising a magic review count that "guarantees" the map pack; there's no such threshold), Craigslist services posts in the labor/move category (those run about $5 each in the US - cheap, not free), and a free Facebook business Page you share into local buy/sell groups while answering "ISO a hauler" posts. Note: don't list your service as an item on Facebook Marketplace - services get removed there. More tactics in how to get junk removal customers.

4. Raising prices when you're booked out

The single clearest signal of a profitable operator: when the calendar fills, they raise rates. If you're turning away work, your prices are too low, full stop. A 10-15% bump on a booked-solid schedule drops almost entirely to the bottom line. Broke operators stay cheap out of fear and stay busy-but-poor forever.

So - is it worth starting?

If you'll do the unglamorous parts - quote fast, price flat, chase property-manager accounts, mind your dump fees, and raise prices when demand says to - junk removal is one of the more profitable low-startup-cost service businesses out there. The margins are genuinely good. What separates the operators making real money from the ones spinning their wheels isn't the truck. It's the system.
