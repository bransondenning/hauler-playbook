Junk Removal Franchise vs Independent: An Honest Cost & Control Breakdown

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Every week somebody messages me asking the same thing: should I buy a junk removal franchise, or just start my own truck? I run an actual junk removal company, so I'm going to give you the version nobody selling a franchise will. This is the honest breakdown of junk removal franchise vs independent — the fees, the royalties, the control, the brand, and where the money actually goes.

Short version: a franchise can shortcut your learning curve and hand you a recognizable name. But it does that by taking a permanent slice of every dollar you earn, forever. For most people reading this, the independent path keeps more money in your pocket and more decisions in your hands. Here's the math.

What you're actually buying with a franchise

A junk removal franchise sells you a system: a brand customers recognize, a booking/dispatch platform, training, and a protected territory. That has real value, especially if you've never run a service business. But understand what it costs, because the sticker price is the smallest part.

In 2026, expect a franchise to involve most or all of these:

  • Initial franchise fee: roughly $40,000 to $90,000 for a single territory, depending on the brand and market size.
  • Total startup investment: commonly $90,000 to $250,000+ once you add trucks, branded wraps, uniforms, working capital, and required reserves.
  • Royalty: typically 7% to 10% of gross revenue — not profit, gross. You pay this on every job whether you made money on it or not.
  • Brand/marketing fund: usually another 1% to 3% of gross on top of the royalty.
  • Required tech/software fees, ongoing training, and renewal fees at the end of your term.

Put real numbers on it. Say you build a solid single-truck operation doing $300,000 in gross revenue in a good year. At a combined 10% in royalties and brand fees, that's $30,000 a year off the top — every year you operate. Over ten years, that's $300,000 you handed up the chain. That's the part the discovery-day presentation glosses over.

Now the other side. Starting independent, your real barrier to a first paid job is a tow vehicle, a way to haul, and a phone. The legal and insurance reality (don't skip this)

One myth I have to kill every time: an LLC is not a license. An LLC is a liability structure. You may also need a local business license, and depending on your state, sales tax registration. Separate things.

On insurance, carry general liability — budget roughly $40 to $90 a month for a small operation — and get commercial auto on the vehicle you haul with. Your personal auto policy will not cover a vehicle used for hauling junk for pay, and finding that out after an accident is a business-ending mistake. We cover this properly in the junk removal license and insurance guide.

One more compliance item that catches new haulers: refrigerant appliances — fridges, freezers, AC units, dehumidifiers. The refrigerant must be recovered by an EPA-certified technician using proper equipment. Never cut the lines or vent it. Either get certified, partner with a recovery shop, or charge for the appliance accordingly and route it correctly.

Control: the part that decides everything

Money matters, but control is where independents really win. As a franchisee, you operate inside someone else's rulebook. That can mean mandated pricing structures, approved-vendor requirements, restrictions on what services you can add, territory boundaries you can't cross, and approval processes for changes you'd otherwise make in an afternoon.

As an independent, you set your own prices. You can run a Saturday-only junk removal side hustle or scale to a fleet — your call. You decide your price card and adjust it the day fuel or dump fees move. You can chase property manager cleanout accounts and estate cleanout work that fits your market, not a corporate playbook written for the average territory.

Brand: real advantage, but smaller than they sell it

The strongest franchise pitch is brand recognition — a name people already trust when a truck pulls up. That is genuinely worth something on the first call. But here's what they don't emphasize: at the local level, you build trust the same way they do, just slower at first and free.

What actually wins local junk removal customers in 2026:

  • A Google Business Profile with real photos and a steady stream of reviews. Ask every happy customer to leave a review from their own phone right after the job — that's the single biggest local-ranking lever, and it costs you nothing.
  • A clean, simple website with your service area and a phone number that you actually answer.
  • A Facebook Page plus participation in local buy/sell and community groups. Note: Facebook Marketplace forbids listing services as items, so don't try to list "junk removal" as a product there — use your Page and post in local groups instead.
  • A $5 Craigslist post in the labor/move category (Craigslist service ads are paid, not free — budget the few dollars and repost as it ages off).

Do that consistently and within a few months your own name carries weight in your town. We laid out the whole playbook in how to get junk removal customers and junk removal marketing ideas. A good business name helps too — and you own it instead of renting it.

So is independent actually more profitable?

For most operators, yes — primarily because you keep the royalty. Let me frame this carefully: the numbers below are examples, not promises. Your results depend on your market, your hustle, your pricing, and how lean you run. Nobody can guarantee income in this business.

As an example, a single-truck independent doing eight to ten jobs a week at an average ticket in the $250–$450 range can land somewhere around $150,000 to $300,000 in gross revenue in a healthy year. Your costs — fuel, dump/transfer fees, insurance, vehicle upkeep, and labor — eat a real chunk of that. But the difference between paths is stark: the franchise version of that same revenue quietly loses $15,000 to $30,000 a year to royalties and brand fees that the independent simply keeps.

That doesn't make franchising wrong for everyone. If you want a turnkey system, have the capital, and value a proven brand over maximum margin, a franchise can be a fine choice. But if you're reading an operator's blog looking for the smart money move, the independent path usually wins on cost, control, and long-term profit. We go deeper on the math in is a junk removal business profitable.

If you go independent, here's the order of operations

  1. Pick a name and register an LLC (remember: that's structure, not a license).
  2. Get your local business license and sales tax registration if your state requires it.
  3. Bind general liability and commercial auto insurance before your first paid job.
  4. Secure a hauling vehicle — used truck or trailer — and a basic dump/transfer plan.
  5. Stand up a Google Business Profile, a one-page website, and a Facebook Page.
  6. Build a volume-based price card so you quote consistently.
  7. Post the $5 Craigslist labor/move ad and start asking every customer for a review.

That's the same sequence we walk through in how to start a junk removal business and the junk removal business plan. None of it requires permission from a corporate office.

The bottom line

A franchise buys you speed and a name and charges you a permanent percentage for it. Going independent costs you more learning up front and a slower brand build — but you keep every dollar you earn and every decision you make. For most haulers, that trade is worth it. Start lean, get insured properly, price right, and let your own reviews build the brand you actually own.
