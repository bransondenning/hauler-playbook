Junk Removal Pricing Guide: How to Price Jobs With a Flat-Rate Load Card

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

Pricing is where new junk removal operators lose the most money. Not on the trailer, not on insurance - on quoting. Quote too low and you grind through a full Saturday that grosses $40 an hour and nets $18 after the dump fee. Quote too high with no system behind it, and you lose the job to the hauler who texted back a clean flat price in ten minutes.

We run a junk removal company, and this article is the exact pricing system we use on real jobs: flat rates by trailer fraction, quoted from a photo, with a short list of add-ons for the stuff that breaks the model. It fits on one card, and it prices about 90% of residential jobs without you ever driving out to look at a pile.

Here's the whole playbook.

Why Hourly Pricing Fails in Junk Removal

The first instinct for most new haulers is to charge by the hour - $75, $100, $125 an hour plus dump fees. It feels fair and it feels safe. It's neither, and here's why:

  • Customers can't budget an hourly rate. "It'll probably take two or three hours, plus disposal" is not a quote, it's a guess the customer has to absorb the risk on. People book the company that gives them one number.
  • Hourly punishes you for being good. The faster and stronger you get, the less you earn per job. You literally pay a tax on your own efficiency. Flat-rate flips that: finish a $275 half-load in 50 minutes and your effective rate just went up.
  • Hourly invites negotiation on-site. Customers watch the clock, question your pace, and ask you to "round it down." A flat price agreed up front ends that conversation before it starts.
  • Dump fees become a fight. Passing through disposal costs means showing receipts, explaining tonnage rates, and arguing about weight. Bake disposal into a flat rate and nobody ever asks.
  • You can't quote remotely. Hourly pricing forces in-person estimates, which means unpaid windshield time. Flat-rate by volume is the only model that works with photo quotes - and photo quotes are how you win jobs in 2026.

Every big franchise in this industry - the ones charging $600 to $800 for a full truck - prices by volume, not time. They didn't pick that model by accident. You should use the same model with a leaner cost structure underneath it, which is exactly how an independent operator undercuts them by 30% and still keeps better margins.

Flat-Rate Pricing by Load Fraction: The System

The whole system rests on one question: how much of my trailer does this job fill? You divide your trailer into fractions - quarter, half, three-quarter, full - and assign a flat price to each. Disposal, fuel, and labor are baked in. The customer gets one number and that number doesn't move unless the pile grows.

Here's a realistic example card for a solo or two-person operation running a 6x12 dump trailer (roughly 12 cubic yards with 4-foot sides) in a mid-cost US market in 2026. Adjust the numbers for your market - high-cost metros run 20-40% above this, rural areas a bit below - but keep the structure. Two things about the shape of this card matter more than the exact dollars:

  • The minimum is high on purpose. $150 to haul one couch sounds steep until you do the math: 30-45 minutes of drive time, loading, a dump run, a gate fee, fuel, and insurance. A $75 minimum is how haulers go broke one couch at a time. Customers who balk at $150 for a single item were never going to be profitable customers.
  • The price per cubic yard drops as the load grows. A quarter load runs about $58 per yard on this card; a full load runs about $35. That's intentional. Your fixed costs (drive out, dump run) are the same either way, so bigger jobs can carry a lower rate and still net more. It also nudges customers to "add the stuff in the shed too" - the easiest upsell in the business.

How to Build Your Own Price Card: The Math

Don't copy anyone's card blind, including ours. Build it from your actual cost stack. Three numbers drive everything:

1. Disposal cost per load

Call your nearest landfill or transfer station and get the tipping fee. In 2026, most US facilities charge somewhere between $60 and $120 per ton, with $25-40 minimum gate fees; Northeast and West Coast metros run higher. A full mixed household load in a 12-yard trailer typically weighs 1,500-2,500 pounds, so plan on roughly $50-$130 to dump a full trailer depending on your region.

2. Operating cost per job

Fuel for the round trip plus the dump run (call it $15-25), plus a per-job slice of insurance, registration, maintenance, and tires. If your annual fixed costs are $6,000 and you run 300 jobs a year, that's $20 a job whether you think about it or not. Most new operators don't think about it. (If you haven't built this stack yet, our junk removal startup costs breakdown walks through every line item.)

3. Your target labor rate

Decide what your time is worth loaded - we suggest building the card so a typical job pays you $80-120 per labor hour gross before overhead. That sounds high until you remember it has to cover slow weeks, quoting time, and your back.

Now run a real example. A half-trailer job at $275:

  • Disposal: roughly 900 lbs at $80/ton, plus gate minimum - call it $45
  • Fuel and vehicle costs: $20
  • Fixed-cost allocation: $20
  • Margin left over: about $190 on a job that takes 1.5-2 hours door to door, including the dump run

That's an effective $95-125 per hour gross for a solo operator - typical for a well-priced job, not a guarantee, and it assumes you're not driving 40 minutes each way. Run this same math on every line of your card. If any fraction nets you less than your target labor rate on a realistic time estimate, raise that line.

One more rule we live by: when you're booked solid two weeks out, your card is too cheap. Raise every line 10% and watch what happens. Usually: nothing, except your margins.

The Photo-Quote System: Price Jobs Without Driving Anywhere

The flat-rate card only reaches full power when you pair it with photo quotes. The pitch to the customer is one sentence, and it's the best marketing line in the industry:

"Text us a photo of the pile and we'll text back a flat price within the hour."

No appointment, no stranger walking through their house, no "estimate window." Just a picture and a number. Here's the workflow we run:

  1. Customer texts photos. Ask for one wide shot and one angle shot. Two photos catch most surprises.
  2. You estimate the trailer fraction. With a few weeks of reps this takes under a minute (rules of thumb below).
  3. You text back a flat price with the terms. Example script: "Thanks for the photos! That's right at a half-trailer load - flat price is $275, which includes all labor, hauling, and disposal. Price is locked as long as the pile matches the photos. We can do Thursday 10am or Saturday 8am - which works?"
  4. Price is locked unless the pile grows. If you show up and there's a second pile in the garage, you re-quote on the spot using the same card - "no problem, that bumps us to three-quarter, so $350 total." The card makes the adjustment feel like math instead of a shakedown.

Calibrating your eye is the only skill here, and it comes fast. Useful anchors:

  • A standard 3-seat sofa ≈ one-eighth of a 12-yard trailer
  • A fridge, washer, or dryer ≈ one-tenth each
  • A level-full 8-foot pickup bed ≈ a quarter trailer
  • 10-12 full contractor bags ≈ one-eighth
  • A single-car garage packed waist-high ≈ a full trailer, sometimes more

When in doubt between two fractions, quote the higher one and tell the customer you'll drop the price on-site if it loads smaller. You'll keep the job either way, and the occasional "good news, it came in at $275 instead of $350" call builds more five-star reviews than any discount ever will. Speed matters more than precision here - the first company to put a real number in the customer's hands wins the booking more often than the cheapest one does, which is a core theme in our guide on how to get junk removal customers.

Add-Ons: When the Card Isn't Enough

Volume pricing assumes normal household junk - furniture, boxes, appliances, yard bags. A handful of job types break that assumption, and they're exactly where new haulers get hurt. Handle them with published add-ons, not improvisation.

Heavy material: price by weight, never by volume

Concrete, dirt, brick, tile, sod, and roofing shingles are the classic trap. A quarter trailer of mixed furniture weighs maybe 400 pounds; a quarter trailer of concrete weighs 2,500-3,000 and can exceed your axle rating before it looks half full. Two rules: cap heavy material at a quarter trailer per trip, and price it on its own line - typical 2026 numbers run $150-$250 per quarter-trailer of clean heavy debris depending on your local per-ton tipping rate. If a customer mixes a shingle pile into a furniture job, quote it as two line items.

Access surcharges: stairs and long carries

Your card assumes ground-floor access with the trailer reasonably close. Charge for anything else:

  • Stairs: +$25-50 per flight above or below ground level. A third-floor walk-up couch is a different job than a curbside couch.
  • Long carry: +$25-75 when you can't get the trailer within ~100 feet of the junk (backyard sheds, gated complexes, basements with exterior-only access).

Specialty items with their own flat price

  • Hot tubs: $300-500 flat. Plan on two people, cutting it into quarters with a reciprocating saw, and about two hours. Quoted as its own job, not a trailer fraction - and it's one of the most profitable jobs in the industry once you've done a few.
  • Pianos: $200-400. Uprights at the low end, anything bigger at the high end, plus stair fees.
  • Refrigerant appliances: +$25-40 to cover the freon-recovery surcharge most facilities charge.
  • Mattresses and box springs: +$15-40 each in states and counties with per-unit disposal fees - check yours before you eat the cost.
  • CRT TVs and e-waste: +$20-35 each where landfills refuse them and you're routing to a recycler.
  • Tires: +$10-20 each.
  • Paint, chemicals, and hazmat: quote case-by-case or decline. Know what your facility accepts before you load it, not after.

Print these on the back of the card. When the add-on is written down before the customer asks, it's a policy. When you invent it on their driveway, it's a negotiation.

Pricing for Property Managers and Repeat Commercial Work

Everything above covers one-off residential jobs. The other half of a healthy junk removal business is repeat work - property managers, realtors, landlords - and it prices differently because the value exchange is different: they give you volume and predictability, you give them priority and a discount.

The offer we built our commercial side on is simple: 20% off the card, for life, for property-management accounts. A half-trailer unit cleanout drops from $275 to $220. You make less per load and far more per month, because one mid-size property manager can send 2-6 cleanouts a month - and because their real pain point isn't your price, it's vacancy days. Every day a unit sits full of an ex-tenant's furniture is a day of lost rent, so the manager who can text you photos at 9am and have the unit empty by 2pm stops shopping on price entirely. The photo-quote system slots perfectly into this: their leasing agent texts unit photos, you reply with the discounted flat price, done. Five Pricing Mistakes That Kill New Haulers

  1. Pricing against the dump fee instead of the full cost stack. "The dump only charges $40, so $120 is great money" ignores fuel, insurance, maintenance, quoting time, and the drive. Build the card from all three cost numbers or you're guessing.
  2. Doing free in-person estimates. Two drive-outs a day for quotes is 8-10 unpaid hours a week. The photo quote exists to kill this. Reserve on-site estimates for hoarding-level cleanouts and commercial bids only.
  3. Setting the minimum too low. The $75-minimum hauler and the $150-minimum hauler do the same single-couch job. One of them is still in business in 18 months.
  4. Caving on price at the door. If the price was locked from photos, there's nothing to discuss. If the pile grew, the card re-quotes it for you. The moment customers learn your prices bend, every job becomes a haggle.
  5. Racing the gig-app guys to the bottom. There will always be someone with a borrowed pickup doing loads for $60. They're not your competition; they're uninsured, unreliable, and gone by next quarter. Compete with the franchises on price and with the gig guys on professionalism - licensed, insured, on time, flat price in writing.

Get the card right, lock prices from photos, publish your add-ons, and discount only in exchange for volume. That's the entire pricing engine - the same one running in our trucks right now. Price with a system and every other part of the business gets easier: marketing writes itself ("text a photo, get a flat price"), scheduling tightens up, and you stop leaving $100 on driveways.
