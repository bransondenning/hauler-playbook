Hoarding Cleanout Service: How to Run and Price This Niche (2026)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

We run a junk removal company, and a hoarding cleanout service is the most misunderstood, highest-paying corner of this trade. One job can fill six to ten trailer loads, run two or three days, and ticket out at five figures. It is also the job most haulers quote wrong, walk into unprepared, and walk out of having lost money. The work is sensitive, the conditions are genuinely hazardous, and the buyer is rarely the person who created the mess.

This is the operator's playbook for adding hoarding cleanouts to a hauling business: the safety and PPE baseline, how to price a job you cannot eyeball, how to work with the families, social workers, and realtors who feed you the leads, and the firm list of what to refuse. If you are starting from zero, read how to start a junk removal business first for the legal and equipment basics, then come back here for the niche. It pairs closely with the estate cleanout business work, but hoarding jobs carry risks an empty inherited house never will.

Why a hoarding cleanout service pays more than any other junk job

A standard pickup is one customer, one decision, one load. A hoarding cleanout is a whole house packed floor to ceiling, sometimes with only goat-paths to walk through, hidden hazards under every pile, and a level of labor that doubles or triples your time per cubic yard. You are not hauling furniture out of empty rooms. You are excavating.

That difficulty is exactly why it pays. In our experience, a moderate hoarding cleanout in a typical US market runs $2,000 to $8,000, and severe cases with biohazard or structural issues go well past $15,000. Those are example ranges from real jobs, not a promise of what you will earn; your market, your volume, and your willingness to take the hard ones decide that. Compare it to a $150 minimum pickup and the math is obvious. You do not need many of these a month. Safety and PPE: this is the part you cannot skip

Most haulers treat PPE as optional. On a hoarding job it is the difference between a profitable week and a hospital visit. These houses hide ammonia from animal waste, mold spores thick enough to see in the light, rodent droppings that carry hantavirus, used needles, rotting food, and air that has not moved in years. Walk in unprotected and you are gambling your lungs and your crew's.

The baseline kit we will not start a hoarding job without:

  • Respirators, not dust masks. A half-face respirator with P100 cartridges, not a paper mask. Budget $30 to $40 per respirator and a few dollars per cartridge pair. Anyone on the crew who cannot get a clean seal does not go in.
  • Nitrile gloves under cut-resistant work gloves. The nitrile keeps biological contamination off your skin; the outer glove handles the sharp edges and broken glass buried in every pile.
  • Disposable Tyvek-style coveralls and boot covers. Roughly $5 to $12 a suit. You peel them off and bag them at the end of the day so you are not carrying the house home in your truck cab.
  • Eye protection and a first-aid kit on the truck. Sealed goggles, not safety glasses, in rooms with airborne contamination.
  • A sharps container and heavy contractor bags. Needles and broken glass get isolated, not tossed loose into the trailer.

Build the full PPE cost into the quote as a line item, the same way you bill dump fees. A few hundred dollars of consumables on a $5,000 job is nothing, and customers do not blink when you explain you are showing up properly equipped. The crews that skimp here are the ones who burn out, get sick, and quit the niche inside a year.

Where hoarding cleanout leads actually come from

The person with the hoard almost never calls you. The shame is too heavy, and many of them do not see the problem the way the people around them do. Your leads come from the professionals trying to help that person, and your job is to be the calm, discreet name those people keep in their phone. In rough order of payoff:

  • Social workers, case managers, and Adult Protective Services. When a home becomes a health or eviction risk, these are the people racing a deadline. They refer constantly and they refer the same trusted vendor every time. Be gentle, be reliable, never judge their client out loud, and you become that vendor.
  • Realtors and listing agents. A hoarder property is a dead listing until it is empty. Clear one fast and clean and you are their permanent cleanout person. One productive agent can send a job a month.
  • Property managers and landlords. Eviction and turnover cleanouts on a packed unit are a recurring need. This is the same B2B muscle behind landing property-manager cleanout accounts, and the exact same outreach works on every source on this list.
  • Probate and elder-law attorneys. Executors clearing a hoarder estate hand the job to the vendor who makes them look good.
  • Professional organizers and therapists who specialize in hoarding disorder. They handle the sorting and the emotional side; they hire someone else to haul. That should be you.

The outreach is simple but it takes effort: a short intro email, a follow-up text, and an in-person drop-by beat any ad. You still want a consumer-facing presence so families who do search can find you. A complete Google Business Profile with "hoarding cleanout" in your services, plus a local Facebook page and a few community-group touches, covers it. One accuracy note that trips up new operators: Facebook Marketplace forbids listing services as items, so do not try to post a cleanout listing there. Use a Facebook business page and local groups instead. For the cheap-but-real channel, a Craigslist post in the labor/move category costs $5 (it is not free) and still pulls residential calls. How to price a hoarding cleanout

We price almost everything by flat rate based on how much of the trailer the job fills, not by the hour. Hourly invites haggling and punishes you for being efficient. Here is our standard load card for normal junk work:

Load sizeExample flat rate
Minimum pickup$150
Quarter trailer$175
Half trailer$275
Three-quarter trailer$350
Full trailer$425

These are example figures from our market, all-in (labor, hauling, dump fees, and sweep-out). Your local dump rates will move them. For a full walkthrough of building your own card, see our junk removal pricing guide. A hoarding job, though, is not just stacked loads. It is the one job where you must adjust for difficulty, or you will lose money on volume that looks the same as easy junk but takes three times as long to move.

How we quote the hard ones:

  • Never quote sight unseen, and never from a single photo. Walk the whole house, or get a full video tour. The garage, the back bedroom, the crawlspace, and the corner nobody mentions always hold more than the front rooms suggest.
  • Estimate total trailer loads, then add a difficulty multiplier. A house packed to the ceiling with narrow paths can take twice the labor per load of a clean estate. We will mark a severe job up 50% to 100% over straight volume pricing because of the dig-out time, the PPE, and the slower, careful handling.
  • Bundle into one number, but stage the payment. Quote "$6,400 to clear the entire house, everything out, floors swept." On multi-day jobs we take a deposit and bill per day or per phase so we are never floating thousands in dump fees on a handshake.
  • Build in the add-ons. Stairs, long carries, and packed-out conditions are real labor. A typical stairs or long-carry add runs $30 to $50 per flat. Refrigerant appliances, mattresses, TVs, and tires carry small per-item fees because they cost you extra at the scale.

Working with families, social workers, and realtors

This is the part that wins the job over a cheaper competitor, and the part most haulers get wrong. You are not clearing a garage. You are inside the most private, most shameful corner of someone's life, often while a family is exhausted and a person with a real disorder is watching their possessions leave. How you show up matters as much as your price.

Three things separate the operators who own this niche:

You stay calm and you never judge. No comments, no faces, no jokes to your crew. The social worker who hired you is watching how you treat their client, and the family will remember whether you made a hard day worse. We have earned referrals from a single case manager simply by being the crew that treated her client like a person.

You watch for what matters and flag it before it goes in the trailer. Hoards bury cash, important documents, jewelry, medication, and irreplaceable keepsakes inside the trash. Set anything that looks personal or valuable aside and hand it to the responsible party. On a big job, get the decision in writing. This is non-negotiable: in a hoard, the family or the client decides what is trash, not you.

You donate what you can and get the receipt. Build relationships with a couple of charities and tell the customer up front: "Anything usable, we donate, and we'll get you the receipt." That receipt has real value to an estate, and the gesture often tips the decision your way. Put both the respect and the donation angle in your reviews. Speaking of reviews, the best ones come from the customer leaving them on their own phone, in the moment, before you drive off. Never log into anyone's account for them.

What to refuse, flag, or charge extra for

Knowing your limit protects you legally, financially, and physically. The customer or the case worker will sometimes push you to "just take it all." On these items, you do not:

  • Refrigerators, freezers, and AC units. These contain refrigerant that federal law requires an EPA-certified technician to recover before scrapping. Never cut a refrigerant line yourself. Partner with a certified recycler and charge a $25 to $50 line item.
  • Hazardous materials. Paint, solvents, motor oil, propane tanks, pesticides, and pool chemicals are not regular trash and most transfer stations refuse them in your load. Direct the family to the county household hazardous waste facility, which takes them for free.
  • Biohazard, severe mold, and human or animal waste. This is the line that separates junk removal from remediation. Heavy contamination, sewage, or anything requiring structural drying and antimicrobial treatment is a licensed remediation trade, not a hauling job. Know your limit and refer it, or partner with a remediation company and handle only the bulk haul-out after they clear the space.
  • Sharps, medical waste, and unlabeled chemicals. Isolate sharps in a proper container; do not open mystery containers.
  • Firearms, ammunition, and anything that might have value. Stop and get the executor's or family's direction in writing. There are legal handling rules, and you do not want to be the reason a probate gets complicated.

The rule is simple: if it is regulated, dangerous, or possibly valuable, you flag it and quote it separately. That discipline is exactly what makes social workers and attorneys trust you with bigger jobs.

The legal and insurance baseline

Before you take money for clearing anyone's home, get the basics right. Carry general liability insurance, which typically runs $40 to $90 a month for a small hauling operation, because you are working inside people's homes and around real hazards. You also need commercial auto on the truck, because personal auto policies exclude business hauling and will deny a claim on a paid load. Form an LLC for liability protection, but understand an LLC is not a license. Many cities require a separate business license or hauler permit, and you will need a transfer-station account to dispose legally. Check your local rules. A realistic first 90 days in the niche

  1. Lock the basics: general liability and commercial auto insurance, your city license, a transfer-station account, and a trailer that swallows full loads.
  2. Buy and stock the PPE kit so you can walk any house the day they call.
  3. Build the flat-rate card and a difficulty multiplier so you can quote a packed house without underbidding it.
  4. Set up two donation outlets and learn what they will and will not accept.
  5. Make your referral list: five social workers or case managers, ten realtors, three property managers, and two probate attorneys. Visit them in person.
  6. Do the first job flawlessly, stay calm, flag the valuables, donate what you can, and ask for the review on the customer's phone before you leave.

A hoarding cleanout service rewards operators who are organized, careful, and genuinely decent under pressure. The price premium is real, but it is earned by handling the hardest day of a family's year better than the next hauler would.
