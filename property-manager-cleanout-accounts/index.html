How to Get Property Management Cleanout Contracts (The Recurring-Revenue Playbook)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

One-off junk removal customers are fine. They pay, they leave a review, and your revenue resets to zero on Monday. A property management account is a different animal: one contact who sends you 12 to 25 paid jobs a year, never haggles after the first job, and refers you to other managers when you perform. If you're figuring out how to get junk removal customers in general, start there - but if you want revenue you can actually predict, this is the channel. Here's exactly how to get property management cleanout contracts, including the offer that gets you in the door and the five-touch outreach cadence we still use in our own junk removal company.

Why property managers are the best customer in junk removal

Run the math on a typical residential customer versus a typical PM account. A homeowner garage cleanout might be a $300 ticket, and it might have cost you $40 in ad spend or weeks of Google ranking effort to win. You may never hear from that customer again.

Now take a mid-size property management firm running 150 doors. Industry turnover on managed rentals typically runs 35-45% a year, so that firm is handling roughly 55-65 move-outs annually. In our experience, somewhere between a fifth and a third of move-outs leave enough behind - furniture, mattresses, trash, the occasional fridge full of science experiments - to need a professional cleanout. That's 12 to 18 jobs a year from a single phone number, at typical tickets of $250-$450, with eviction cleanouts often running $400-$800. Call it $4,000-$7,000 a year per account as a realistic example. Land three accounts and you've built a $15,000-$20,000 baseline before you spend a dollar on marketing.

And it compounds. PMs know other PMs. They know landlords, realtors, and contractors. The first account is the hardest one you'll ever land; the fifth one usually calls you.

PMs don't buy junk removal - they buy vacancy days back

This is the single most important reframe in this article, and it's the reason most haulers' outreach gets ignored.

A property manager does not care about junk. They care about days vacant, because that's the number their owners judge them on. With median US asking rents sitting around $1,600-$1,700 a month in 2026, every day a unit sits empty costs the owner roughly $55. A unit can't be cleaned, painted, photographed, or listed until the previous tenant's stuff is out - which means the cleanout is the bottleneck at the very front of every turn.

If the PM's current process takes four or five days to get a unit emptied (call a hauler, schedule a walkthrough, wait for a quote, approve it, wait for a crew), and you can have it broom-swept in 24-48 hours from a text message, you just handed them three to four vacancy days back - $165-$220 in recovered rent per turn, plus a faster turn metric to show their owners. Your job partially pays for itself before they've even looked at your price.

So your pitch is never "we haul junk." Your pitch is: "We get your units rent-ready faster." Every script below is built on that sentence.

The offer that opens doors: photo quotes, no walkthroughs, ever

Here's the friction point nobody talks about. The person who hires haulers at a PM firm - usually a maintenance coordinator or portfolio manager - is juggling 20-40 open work orders at any given moment. The last thing they want to do is drive across town to meet a hauler at a unit so he can scratch his chin and produce a price. Walkthroughs cost them an hour they don't have.

So you remove the walkthrough entirely. The offer that has landed every PM account we have looks like this:

  • Photo quote, flat price, within the hour. They text you 3-5 photos of the unit, you reply with a firm written number - not an estimate, not a range - usually within the hour during business hours. No walkthrough, no waiting.
  • 20% off our flat-rate card, for life. Not a one-time promo. A permanent preferred rate that makes switching away from you feel expensive.
  • Scheduled within 24-48 hours of approval, with arrival-window texts.
  • Before-and-after photos texted same day, so they can forward proof to the owner without driving out. You're not just cleaning units - you're making the PM look good to their boss.
  • Net-30 invoicing with one invoice per unit, property address in the subject line, W-9 and certificate of insurance already on file.

Notice what this offer really is: it's not a discount, it's a process upgrade. You've turned a half-day chore into a two-minute text thread. That's what they're buying.

Build your hit list before you send a single text

Open Google Maps, search "property management" in your metro, and build a list of 20 firms. Prioritize companies managing roughly 50-300 doors: big enough to have steady turnover, small enough that they don't route vendors through a national procurement portal. Skip the giant REIT-owned communities at first - their vendor onboarding can take months and pay slow.

Round out the list with HOA management companies, realtors who manage rentals on the side, mobile home park managers, self-storage facilities (abandoned and auctioned units), and - if you're in a college town - any landlord with student housing, because the May-to-August turn season is a gold rush.

Then find the right human. The decision-maker is almost never the broker or owner whose face is on the website. It's the maintenance coordinator or property manager - the person drowning in work orders. One call to the front desk gets you the name: "Hi, quick question - who handles your unit turns and move-out cleanouts?"

How to get property management cleanout contracts: the 5-touch cadence

Here's the part most haulers get wrong. They send one email, hear nothing, and conclude "PMs aren't interested." In our experience, almost no PM account closes on the first touch - and almost no competitor ever makes a third. The cadence below is designed around a simple reality: the PM doesn't need you today. They need you the day their current hauler no-shows during turn season. Your job is to be the obvious next call when that happens.

Touch 1 - The text (day 1)

Text beats email for this audience; maintenance coordinators live on their phones. Keep it under 50 words:

"Hi [Name], this is [You] with [Company]. We do move-out and eviction cleanouts for property managers in [area] - flat-rate photo quotes within the hour, no walkthroughs, units cleared in 24-48 hrs. PMs get 20% off our standard rates, for life. Can I text you our rate card?"

Touch 2 - The follow-up text (day 3-4)

One line, plus proof. Attach a before-and-after photo of a recent cleanout: "[Name], wanted to make sure this didn't get buried - here's a 2-bed eviction we cleared in one afternoon last week. Happy to be a backup vendor if you've already got someone." That "backup vendor" line lowers the stakes and gets more replies than any hard close we've tried.

Touch 3 - The call (day 7-10)

Thirty seconds, standing up, with one goal - not to close, but to confirm you're talking to the right person and plant the vacancy-days pitch: "I'll keep it quick - we help PMs get units rent-ready faster. You text us photos, you get a flat price within the hour, unit's empty in 24-48 hours. Who should I send our rate card and COI to?" If it goes to voicemail, leave exactly that and hang up.

Touch 4 - The drop-in (week 2-3)

This is the touch that converts. Show up at the office, clean shirt, and ask for your contact by name. Leave a one-page rate card, a copy of your certificate of insurance, and business cards for the front desk. Five minutes, no pressure: "Just wanted to put a face to the texts." Our first PM account closed exactly this way - after three ignored texts and an unreturned voicemail, the coordinator looked at the rate card and said, "Honestly, our current guy keeps no-showing." That account has paid us every month since.

Touch 5 - The quarterly ping (forever)

If they don't bite, you don't quit - you go dormant. Every 90 days, one text: a recent before/after photo and one line: "Still doing 24-48 hr turns in [area] if you ever need a backup." It costs you 20 seconds. Vendor failures are a when, not an if - haulers no-show, raise prices mid-season, or quit the business - and when it happens, you want to be the most recent name in their messages. We've landed accounts on the fourth quarterly ping, a full year after first contact.

Pricing PM work: flat rates by trailer fraction

Flat-rate pricing isn't just easier for you - it's what makes the PM relationship work. A flat-rate card lets the coordinator approve jobs without phone tag, budget turns accurately, and even pre-authorize anything under a half-load so your invoice never waits on an owner's signature. Hourly pricing or "it depends" quotes kill PM accounts, because the PM can't approve a number that doesn't exist.

A $280 three-quarter load with no ad spend and no drive across town beats a $350 retail job that cost you $45 in leads. Volume at a slightly thinner margin, forever, is exactly the trade you want.

The operating standards that keep the account

Landing the account is sales. Keeping it is operations. PMs fire vendors for reliability, not price - so these are non-negotiable:

  • Never no-show. If you're running 30 minutes late, text before the window opens, not after it closes.
  • COI before the first job. Have general liability ($1M is the standard ask) with the PM firm listed as certificate holder, ready to send the same day they ask. Fumbling this is the fastest way to lose an account you already won - our license and insurance guide covers what to carry and what it costs.
  • W-9 on file before the first invoice. Their bookkeeper will ask; beat them to it.
  • Same-day before/after photos, every job. This is your retention tool. The PM forwards your photos to the owner as proof of work - you're doing their reporting for them.
  • Respect lockbox protocol. Get the code, lock everything behind you, confirm completion by text with the unit address.
  • Take everything. Don't cherry-pick the easy stuff and leave the nasty fridge. The nasty fridge is why they hired you.
  • Invoice clean. One invoice per unit, property address on it, net-30, no surprises versus the quoted number. Ever.

Mistakes that kill PM outreach

  • Pitching the broker instead of the maintenance coordinator. The owner's name is on the door; the coordinator hires the haulers.
  • Requiring walkthroughs. You've recreated the exact friction you were supposed to remove.
  • Quitting after one touch. The account usually closes between touches three and five - or on a quarterly ping months later.
  • Vague pricing. "Depends what's in there" is not a number a PM can approve.
  • Treating the first job casually. The first job is an audition. Show up early, over-deliver, send the photos before they ask.

What a realistic timeline looks like

From operators we've coached and our own numbers: a 20-firm list, worked through the full five-touch cadence over 90 days, typically generates two to four photo-quote requests and one to three recurring accounts. That's not a guarantee - markets, timing, and execution vary - but it means the realistic cost of a $5,000-a-year account is roughly 10-15 hours of outreach and a box of donuts. There is no paid ad on earth with that return.
