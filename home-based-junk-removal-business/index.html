How to Run a Junk Removal Business From Home (2026 Operator Guide)

By the operators at Peak Business Kits - Updated June 2026

I've been hauling junk for years, and here's the part nobody tells you when you're getting started: you don't need a warehouse, a yard, or a storefront. My first 18 months ran out of my driveway and a single-car garage. If you're trying to figure out how to run a junk removal business from home, the good news is that low overhead is the single biggest advantage you have over the franchise trucks rolling around your town. The bad news is that "from home" comes with a few rules you can't ignore, or you'll get a nasty letter from your HOA before you book your tenth job.

This is the real playbook on the home-based setup: where the rig parks, how to handle zoning and HOA, where to stash resale items, and how to keep your monthly burn near zero so almost every dollar you collect is yours.

Why home-based is the smartest way to start

The math is simple. A commercial yard or shop in most US metros runs $800 to $2,500 a month. Run from home and that number is zero. When your overhead is near nothing, your break-even is a single job. That's the whole reason this model works.

Parking the rig

Whether you run a pickup with a dump insert, a box truck, or a pickup pulling a dump trailer, the first practical problem is where it sleeps. A few realities:

  • Residential streets and HOAs often ban commercial vehicles and trailers from overnight parking, especially anything with lettering or over a certain weight or length. Check your covenants before you wrap the truck.
  • A clean, unmarked truck draws zero attention. Plenty of home-based operators skip the vinyl wrap on day one and use magnetic door signs they pop off at night. It keeps neighbors happy and keeps you flexible.
  • Trailers are easier to hide than trucks. If you're weighing the rig itself, our breakdown of the best trailer for junk removal covers what fits a home setup and tows clean.

If your driveway won't work, a nearby storage facility with drive-up access or an outdoor RV/boat lot will rent you a parking spot for $50 to $150 a month. Still cheaper than a yard, and it solves the HOA problem in one move.

Zoning, HOA, and the "from home" rules

You can absolutely operate the business from a home address. What you can't always do is run heavy operations there. The distinction most cities draw is between an office and a worksite.

What's usually fine

  • Using your home as your business mailing address and where you answer the phone and do quotes.
  • Parking one compliant work vehicle (subject to HOA/city rules above).
  • Storing a reasonable amount of resale inventory inside your garage.

What gets you in trouble

  • Piling junk, scrap, or appliances in the yard or driveway where it's visible. This is the fastest way to a code-enforcement citation.
  • Heavy daily traffic, employees parking on the street, or a small fleet at the curb.
  • Running a transfer-station-style operation where loads sit and get sorted on-site.

The fix is discipline: empty your truck at the landfill, transfer station, or recycler at the end of the route, not at your house. Your home is the office and the parking spot, never the dump pile.

The license-and-LLC confusion

Let me clear this up because new haulers get it wrong constantly. An LLC is not a business license. An LLC is a legal structure that separates your personal assets from the business. A business license (or local business tax registration) is a separate thing your city or county issues so you can legally operate. Most home-based haulers need a home-occupation permit or general business license, plus the LLC if they want liability protection. They are not the same document, and having one does not cover the other. Get both straight before your first paid job. Insurance you actually need

Skipping insurance to save money is how home-based operators lose their house over one dropped sofa. At minimum:

  • General liability: roughly $40 to $90 a month for a one-truck operation. This covers property damage and injury claims on the job. Property managers and commercial clients will not hire you without it.
  • Commercial auto: your personal policy will not cover a vehicle used for hauling. Commercial auto is non-negotiable and typically runs $150 to $300 a month.

That's it to start. Workers' comp comes later when you hire. Don't let an insurance salesperson upsell you a full package on day one.

Storage for resale: where the home model quietly makes you money

Here's the edge most people miss. A big share of what you haul isn't garbage. Solid furniture, tools, exercise equipment, bikes, working appliances, and collectibles all have resale value. When you run from home, you have a place to stage that stuff and flip it instead of paying to dump it.

How to do it without turning your garage into an episode of Hoarders:

  • Be ruthless about what you keep. Only pull items that will sell in 30 days. If it won't move, it goes to the dump. A garage clogged with unsellable junk is a liability, not an asset.
  • Photograph and list same-day. The longer it sits, the less likely it sells.
  • List on a Facebook PAGE and in local buy/sell/trade groups. Important: Facebook Marketplace does not allow you to post your services as item listings, and that rule gets enforced. You list physical resale items in Marketplace, but you build your service presence through a business Page and by participating in local community groups. Keep the two separate.
  • Use Craigslist for both resale items and your service. Note that Craigslist services posts are not free. A services listing costs $5, and junk hauling belongs in the labor/move category, not the free for-sale sections.

Flipping resale can add a few hundred dollars a month on top of your hauling revenue, and the home garage is what makes it possible without renting space. For more on filling your calendar, see how to get junk removal customers.

Appliances and the rules you can't break

You will get called to haul refrigerators, freezers, window AC units, and dehumidifiers. Anything with refrigerant must have that refrigerant recovered by an EPA-certified technician before disposal. You never, ever cut the lines or vent it. Many transfer stations and scrap yards require a recovery tag or charge a fee to handle the recovery for you. Build that cost into your appliance pricing and you stay legal and profitable. Knowing which items carry extra disposal cost is half of good junk removal pricing.

Keeping overhead near zero

The whole point of the home-based model is that almost every dollar you collect drops to your pocket after fuel and dump fees. Protect that:

  1. Don't buy a new truck. A used pickup with a dump trailer gets you to revenue without a payment. Upgrade later, with cash.
  2. Skip the office software stack. A phone, a free Google Business Profile, a one-page website, and a simple invoicing app cover you for the first year.
  3. Price every job to cover the dump fee plus your time. Underpricing is the only way to lose money in this business when your overhead is this low.
  4. Reinvest resale and early profits into a better trailer or marketing, not a lease you don't need yet.

Getting your first jobs from home

You don't need a storefront to look legitimate. You need to show up where people search and where they refer.

  • Google Business Profile: free, and the single most important listing you'll create. Set it up as a service-area business so you don't have to publish your home address. Then ask every happy customer to leave a review from their own phone. Reviews you post for yourself or from your own device get filtered and can get your profile suspended. Real reviews from the customer's own device are what move you up the local map.
  • Craigslist: the $5 services post in labor/move, renewed regularly.
  • Facebook Page plus local groups: answer "anyone know a junk guy?" posts and you'll book work for free.
  • Property managers: tenant turnovers and cleanouts are steady, repeat money. A home-based solo operator with insurance and a clean truck competes great for these. See property manager cleanout accounts for the cold-call approach.

What you can realistically make

I won't promise you a number, because nobody honest can. Income depends on your market, how hard you hustle, and how well you price. As an example, a home-based solo operator running part-time might do 1 to 3 jobs a day at an average ticket of $250 to $450. Run that a few days a week and you can see why people start this as a side hustle and grow it into full-time. Some weeks are slow. Some are packed. Treat any income figure as an example, not a guarantee.

The bottom line

Running a junk removal business from home works because it strips your overhead to almost nothing while keeping every other advantage. Park a compliant rig, respect your zoning and HOA rules, keep the junk pile out of your yard, stage resale in the garage and flip it fast, handle refrigerant appliances by the book, and get both your license and your LLC in order. Do that, and you're a real business the day you book your first job, with margins the franchise trucks would kill for.
